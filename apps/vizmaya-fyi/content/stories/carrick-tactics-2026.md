## Building from the back

The first principle is patience. Middlesbrough's keeper splits the centre-backs, the full-backs push high and wide, and the two holding midfielders stagger so there is always a passing lane into the next line.

Watch the right side. The right centre-back steps out with the ball and feeds the deeper pivot, who immediately turns and finds the number ten between the lines. As that pass travels, the right-back is already overlapping down the touchline and the left winger drops inside — Boro deliberately overload the central channel to drag markers out of position.

By the end of the move the ball has travelled from inside their own box to the edge of the middle third, and three opponents have been bypassed without a single hopeful long ball.

## Into the final third

The same patience turns ruthless once the ball is in the number ten's feet near halfway.

He spreads it wide to the right winger, who has held the touchline to keep the pitch as big as possible. The overlapping right-back screams past him on the outside, dragging the opposing full-back with him and opening the cut-back lane. The winger doesn't force the cross — he waits, then rolls it back across the six-yard line into the path of the striker arriving at the penalty spot.

This is the whole point of building from the back so deliberately: by the time Boro reach the box, the defence is stretched, the cut-back zone is open, and the chance is a tap-in rather than a half-chance.

Two phases, one idea: control the build-up so completely that the finish looks easy.
