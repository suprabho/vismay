# Land to the Tiller

For generations the cultivators of the Kashmir Valley worked land they could never own. A thin stratum of jagirdars, chakdars and other proprietors held the great estates; the families who actually turned the soil paid in crop, in cash and in labour.

The reform movement that gathered force in the 1940s set out to break that arrangement — to abolish the large landed estates and vest the land in the tiller.

---

## 22.75

*Acres — the ceiling proposed for a single holding*

The reform programme proposed a hard cap on how much land any one proprietor could retain — set at 182 kanals, or roughly 22.75 acres. Everything above the ceiling was to be resumed and redistributed.

Crucially, the resumed land was to be transferred to the tillers without compensation to the former owners — a provision that made this one of the most radical land reforms attempted anywhere in the subcontinent.

---

## Naya Kashmir

The intellectual charter for the reform was the *Naya Kashmir* ("New Kashmir") manifesto, which placed "land to the tiller" at the centre of its economic programme. It framed the estate system not merely as inefficient but as the root of rural poverty.

The demand moved from manifesto to statute over the following decade, as the political movement that authored it gained the power to legislate.

## The Abolition Act

The Big Landed Estates Abolition Act gave the programme legal force. It abolished the large estates, fixed the retention ceiling, and barred compensation for the land taken above it.

By severing ownership from the old proprietary class and attaching it to the cultivator, the Act rewrote the basic terms of rural life in the Valley.

## A Radical Template — and Its Limits

The reform became a reference point for land-to-tiller movements elsewhere in India, precisely because it dispensed with compensation and acted at scale.

It also drew lasting criticism: orchards and certain holdings were treated as exceptions, enforcement was uneven, and disputes over resumed land ran for decades. The template was bold; its execution was contested.
