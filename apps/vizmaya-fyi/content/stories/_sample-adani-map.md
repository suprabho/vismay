## A Decade of Deals

Across five sectors — grain silos, airports, renewable energy land, cross-border power, and environmental clearances — the Adani Group accumulated documented contracts and memoranda of understanding totalling well over ₹85,000 crore in a single decade. Each deal arrived in the wake of a rule change. Each rule change had been contested by the government's own technical arms. And in each case, the objections were overruled.

The most striking single fact sits at the centre of the airports story. In 2018–19, with no prior airport operating experience to its name, Adani Enterprises entered a competitive privatisation round and walked away with all six airports on offer — Ahmedabad, Lucknow, Jaipur, Guwahati, Thiruvananthapuram, and Mangaluru — in a single sweep. The Department of Economic Affairs had recommended a two-airport cap per bidder. NITI Aayog had warned that a firm lacking airport technical capacity 'can well jeopardise the project.' Both notes were overruled. No cap was applied.

The airports are only one chapter. Across grain storage, border-zone solar land in the Rann of Kutch, a power plant in Jharkhand contracted to export every unit it generates to Bangladesh, and a disputed environmental appraisal in the Western Ghats, a consistent shape recurs: a safeguard proposed by regulators is removed, a process proceeds, and Adani emerges as the primary — sometimes the sole — beneficiary. Whether that shape is pattern or coincidence is the question this story sets out to examine.

## Five Sectors, One Pattern

Across five unrelated sectors — grain storage, airport privatisation, renewable energy land, cross-border power export, and environmental clearance — an independent fact-check rated four of five underlying claims substantially accurate: a rule was changed, a safeguard was dropped, and the Adani Group was among the primary beneficiaries each time.

The mechanism repeats with striking consistency. In airports, the Department of Economic Affairs recommended on 10 December 2018 that no single bidder receive more than two airports; that note was reportedly not even discussed at the very next day's PPPAC meeting. NITI Aayog warned that a bidder lacking airport technical capacity 'can well jeopardise the project'; the prior-experience requirement was removed anyway. Adani, with zero airport experience, won all six. In grain storage, FCI itself proposed an anti-monopoly clause to prevent contract concentration; NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs intervened in a 2022 meeting to have it dropped. Adani Agri Logistics subsequently secured contracts worth over ₹9,700 crore — part of a two-firm sweep of roughly 78% of total silo capacity. In Kutch, defence border-construction restrictions were relaxed in April–May 2023 after Gujarat reportedly wrote to the PMO; SECI then surrendered its 23,000-hectare parcel, and Adani Green expanded to approximately 19,000 hectares — the single largest share in the 30 GW Khavda park. At Godda, an unprecedented combination of SEZ status, Telegraph Act right-of-way powers, and a grid-connection amendment issued in August 2024 supports the only Indian power plant contracted to export 100% of its output to a foreign country. In the Western Ghats, the clearest documented red flag is a conflict of interest — an Adani adviser sitting on the environmental appraisal committee — rather than anomalously fast final clearances.

In each case the government and Adani offer a defensible rationale: competitive bids, broader policy intent, multi-developer participation, or decisions taken collectively by empowered committees. Those rebuttals matter and are examined in the sections that follow. But the fact-check's own summary conclusion is precise: 'A consistent pattern recurs across sectors — safeguards proposed by the government's own technical arms were removed and Adani benefited — which makes regulatory capture a reasonable interpretation; but because each case individually has a defensible policy explanation, it remains an interpretation rather than a proven fact.' The five case studies ahead test that interpretation against the primary record.

## The Airport Clean Sweep

In November 2018, the Union Cabinet gave in-principle approval to privatise six regional airports — Ahmedabad, Lucknow, Jaipur, Guwahati, Thiruvananthapuram, and Mangaluru — under a single bidding round. Three conditions quietly transformed who could compete: prior airport-operation experience would not be required, no cap would be placed on how many airports a single bidder could win, and the bid criterion would shift from revenue-sharing to a flat per-passenger fee. Together, those three changes meant that a company that had never run an airport could, in a single sweep, acquire all six.

The government's own technical arms raised the alarm in writing. A Department of Economic Affairs note dated 10 December 2018 recommended that 'not more than two airports' go to any single bidder, warning of concentrated capital risk and arguing that spreading awards would enable 'yardstick competition' — a mechanism for benchmarking performance across operators. NITI Aayog questioned the removal of the experience requirement outright, cautioning that a bidder 'lacking sufficient technical capacity can well jeopardise the project.' The DEA note was reportedly not even placed on the agenda of the 11 December 2018 PPP Appraisal Committee meeting held the very next day.

The PPPAC ruled that prior airport experience 'may neither be made a prerequisite for bidding, nor a post-bid requirement.' When bids were opened in February 2019, Adani Enterprises — carrying zero airport-operation experience — submitted the highest per-passenger fee at all six airports and was awarded every one of them. The bid figures were striking. For Ahmedabad, Adani quoted ₹177 per passenger against GMR Group's ₹85. For Lucknow, Adani bid ₹171 per passenger against AMP Capital's ₹139. The sole financial criterion rewarded the most aggressive number; the proposed two-airport cap that would have prevented a clean sweep was never imposed.

The rebuttals from the government are substantive and on record. Civil Aviation Minister Hardeep Singh Puri told Parliament that both the finance ministry and NITI Aayog were 'part of the decision-making process' and that the three relaxations were designed to 'ensure higher competition and give more flexibility to bidders.' Subhash Chandra Garg, who chaired the PPPAC at the time, said: 'The decisions were taken by the PPPAC… I stand by those decisions, but the decisions were not mine alone.' ICRA, the credit-rating agency, noted that Adani's aggressive bids would deliver a windfall to Airports Authority of India — potentially over ₹600 crore per year. Adani maintains no rules were broken and that it simply outbid all rivals.

What the primary records confirm is narrower but harder to dispute: the DEA recommended a two-airport cap; that recommendation was not discussed at the decisive meeting; the cap was never imposed; and the entity that benefited from its absence was the only bidder with no prior experience in running an airport. The airports case remains the best-documented instance in this story — the one where internal government objections, the meeting that did not discuss them, and the outcome that followed are all traceable to primary documents.

## Where the Rupees Flowed

Across five sectors and roughly a decade, a single corporate name surfaces at the centre of each contract cluster. Add up what is measurable — awarded contracts, signed MoUs, land allocations with assigned capacity — and the concentration becomes stark: the Adani Group holds or has held positions worth well over ₹1 lakh crore in aggregate across grain storage, airports, renewable energy, cross-border power, and forest-zone infrastructure.

The single largest confirmed contract pile sits in grain storage. FCI's Hub and Spoke silo modernisation programme — designed to replace ageing godowns with steel silos under public-private partnership — allocated 110 of 134 contracts to just two companies: Adani Agri Logistics and Leap India Food & Logistics. The duopoly covers roughly 77.5% of the 60 lakh metric tonne (LMT) capacity tendered, worth more than ₹16,500 crore in total. Of that, Adani alone holds contracts exceeding ₹9,700 crore — the largest single-company share in any of the five sectors examined.

The airport cluster is smaller in headline rupee value but carries its own weight: six AAI-run airports awarded in the 2018–19 privatisation round, followed by the acquisition of Mumbai's Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport (CSMIA) from the GVK Group in 2020. Per-passenger fee commitments across the six original airports ran as high as ₹177 per passenger at Ahmedabad — more than double rival bids in several cases.

At Khavda in the Rann of Kutch, the relevant unit is land and generating capacity rather than a contract sum. Adani Green holds approximately 19,000 hectares — the single largest developer allocation in what will be a 30 GW, multi-developer renewable energy park. That translates to roughly 9.5 GW of potential installed capacity, more than any other participant including NTPC, GSECL, GIPCL, and SECI.

The Godda power plant in Jharkhand stands apart in character rather than scale. It is the only Indian generating station contracted to export 100% of its output to a neighbouring country — in this case Bangladesh — and received a package of enabling provisions unavailable to any comparable project: Special Economic Zone status, Telegraph Act right-of-way powers for its dedicated transmission corridor, and a targeted August 2024 Ministry of Power amendment that, in practice, applies to Godda alone.

The Western Ghats figure requires the sharpest caveat on the chart. The ₹60,000 crore value attached to Maharashtra's June 2022 MoU with the Adani Group for pumped-storage and related investment in the Ghats represents a *proposed investment commitment at preliminary-approval stage — not an awarded or active contract*. The Expert Appraisal Committee ultimately rejected or returned Adani's flagship expansion proposal; final environmental clearance was never granted for that project. The bar on this chart marks an intention, not a disbursement.

Taken together, the sectors represent a breadth of regulatory domains — food security infrastructure, civil aviation, defence-adjacent land, cross-border energy law, and environmental clearance — that makes coincidence a harder argument to sustain than pattern. Whether the pattern constitutes deliberate regulatory capture or a well-resourced conglomerate successfully navigating each domain on its own terms is the question the evidence raises but does not fully answer.

## The Silo Duopoly

Of all five sectors examined in this investigation, the grain-storage case offers the most explicit paper trail of a safeguard being proposed by the government's own agency — and then removed by other government bodies before the contracts were awarded. The Food Corporation of India's 'Hub and Spoke' silo modernisation programme was conceived as a roughly ₹20,000 crore public-private partnership to replace ageing conventional godowns across North India's wheat and rice corridors with modern steel silos. FCI itself drafted an anti-monopoly clause into the programme's terms, designed to prevent any single company from cornering an undue share of contracts.

The clause never made it into the final tender documents. According to PPP Appraisal Committee files cited by Newslaundry, a 2022 meeting saw NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs — the same two institutions that had raised objections in the airport round — oppose the restriction. Their argument: let market forces prevail. The clause was dropped.

The market forces that then prevailed produced a striking result. Across 134 contracts, Adani Agri Logistics Ltd and Leap India Food & Logistics together won 110 — a haul covering roughly 46.5 lakh metric tonnes (LMT) out of a total 60 LMT capacity, or approximately 77.5 per cent of the programme. Adani alone secured contracts worth over ₹9,700 crore; the combined Adani–Leap India total exceeded ₹16,500 crore. The All India Kisan Sabha described the outcome as 'backdoor' corporatisation of India's food security infrastructure. CPI(M) and CPI(ML) Liberation demanded a Joint Parliamentary Committee inquiry and the reinstatement of the dropped clause.

The government's rebuttal is substantive and cannot be dismissed. FCI's official statement, posted in June 2026, maintained that 'project awards were determined solely based on competitive bidding outcomes and quoted rates,' and noted that three to fifteen firms competed per project. The government further pointed out that Adani's total contracted capacity — approximately 2.82 million tonnes — represents less than half the programme's tendered capacity; and, critically, that Adani 'did not win any project in Phase II,' a fact that genuinely complicates a straightforward favouritism narrative.

What the Phase II outcome does not resolve is the prior question: why was a safeguard, drafted by the nodal agency responsible for India's grain reserves, removed at the insistence of bodies whose mandate is economic policy rather than food security? The anti-monopoly clause was not a procedural oddity — it was a deliberate design choice by FCI to prevent the very concentration that subsequently occurred. That removal predictably enabled a duopoly regardless of whether every subsequent bid was, in isolation, competitive. The pattern here — technical safeguard proposed, overruled by NITI Aayog and DEA, single beneficiary emerges — is the same institutional sequence documented in the airport privatisation round three years earlier.

## Border Land and the Rann of Kutch

The Rann of Kutch is one of the most inhospitable landscapes on earth — a vast salt flat that bleaches white in the dry season, straddling the India–Pakistan border in northern Gujarat. It is also the site of what will become one of the world's largest renewable energy parks: a 30-gigawatt, multi-developer complex covering roughly 72,600 hectares, or about 445 square kilometres. The geography that makes it exceptional for wind and solar also made it legally inaccessible — until the spring of 2023.

The land at Khavda sits inside a defence-restricted zone. Under longstanding military construction rules, building within several kilometres of the international border required Ministry of Defence clearance that, in practice, was not forthcoming for commercial energy infrastructure. That changed with unusual speed. On 21 April 2023, defence-ministry officials convened a meeting with Army representatives, officials from the renewable-energy ministry, and Gujarat state officials. The meeting produced a consensus: wind turbines would be permitted from one to two kilometres of the border; hybrid solar-wind plants from two to eight kilometres. Within three weeks — on 8 May 2023 — the Ministry of Defence circulated new guidelines formally relaxing the border-construction rules, extending the liberalised regime to all five land borders: Pakistan, China, Bangladesh, Nepal, and Myanmar.

The speed of the policy shift had a documented political antecedent. Gujarat had reportedly written to the Prime Minister's Office in April 2023 — weeks before the defence ministry meeting — asking that the restriction be raised with the MoD. The Guardian, citing sources familiar with the process, reported that the BJP 'lobbied at the highest levels for the protocols to be relaxed to make land in the Rann of Kutch available for both solar and wind construction.' The government's framing of the relaxation as a general infrastructure policy — applicable to all borders and all developers — is factually accurate; the new rules did apply nationally, not solely to Khavda.

What happened next is where the sequence becomes pointed. The Solar Energy Corporation of India had been allotted the largest single parcel in the Khavda park: 23,000 hectares. SECI attended the April 2023 meeting and received the relaxation that had ostensibly been the barrier to development. Then, in July 2023, SECI wrote to Gujarat authorities seeking to surrender its entire allotment. The state accepted the return. The land was taken back into the state's pool. Adani Green Energy subsequently expanded its footprint within the park to approximately 19,000 hectares — equivalent to roughly 9.5 gigawatts of planned capacity — the single largest holding among all developers in the complex.

The park is, genuinely, a multi-developer project. NTPC holds capacity of approximately 4,750 megawatts; Gujarat State Electricity Corporation Limited and Gujarat Industries Power Company hold further allocations; SECI retains a separate parcel; and Suzlon's Sarjan Realties is also present. The nodal agency is the Gujarat Power Corporation Limited, a state entity. The presence of large public-sector developers is a legitimate counter to the framing that Khavda was simply privatised land handed to a single conglomerate.

The counter-narrative, however, does not dissolve the central anomaly: SECI surrendered land that had just become commercially viable — land whose development barrier had been removed in a process that Gujarat had escalated to the PMO. The government has not offered a public explanation for why a state-owned energy corporation would relinquish 23,000 hectares of newly unlocked, strategically located renewable-energy land immediately after the restriction that blocked its use was lifted. It is that sequence — restriction relaxed, public-sector holder retreats, private developer expands — that drives the sharpest unresolved question in this case.

## Godda: India's Only 100% Export Plant

Tucked into Jharkhand's Godda district, Adani Power's 1,600 MW coal-fired plant holds a status unmatched anywhere in India: every unit of electricity it generates is contracted to cross an international border, flowing south-east through a dedicated transmission line to Bangladesh. No other Indian power plant operates under this arrangement — a fact that is not disputed by any party in the debate.

Three layers of regulatory privilege, each documented in official records, underpin the plant's operation. First, Godda was granted Special Economic Zone status — an industrial classification with no precedent for a captive cross-border power facility in India. Second, the plant was accorded right-of-way powers under the Telegraph Act, enabling Adani to lay the dedicated transmission line without the usual easement constraints that apply to private developers. Third, a clause in the original Ministry of Power guidelines of December 2018 — clause 8.9 of the Cross-Border Electricity Guidelines — already permitted Indian generating stations supplying power exclusively to a neighbouring country to build such dedicated lines. Critics who have argued this clause was quietly inserted later are factually incorrect: primary-document verification confirms the provision was present from the Guidelines' original issue date of 18 December 2018. A 3 July 2019 addendum amended only clause 5.2(a) and explicitly left clause 8.9 untouched.

The most consequential regulatory change came later. On 12 August 2024, a Ministry of Power office memorandum amended both clauses 5.2(a) and 8.9, adding a proviso that the government 'may permit' a dedicated-line plant to also connect to the national grid — a co-connection option that did not previously exist. Because Godda is the only plant in India operating under the exclusive-export dedicated-line framework, the amendment, in practice, applies to one facility.

The government's position is that the August 2024 amendment is a general policy update to the cross-border electricity architecture, not a project-specific intervention. Officials have pointed out that the guidelines govern all potential future cross-border generation arrangements, not only Godda, and that granting co-connection flexibility could, in principle, benefit any future exclusive-export plant built by any developer.

'Godda is, factually, the only Indian plant contracted to export 100% of its output to a neighbouring country.' That baseline — undisputed across the fact-check — is what gives each of the three privilege layers its singular quality. Whether the SEZ designation, the Telegraph Act powers, and the 2024 grid amendment represent policy tailored to one group, or the straightforward regulatory scaffolding required to make a novel cross-border energy compact work, is precisely the question the facts alone cannot settle.

## Pattern or Coincidence? You Decide

Five sectors. Five rule changes. One recurring beneficiary. Taken individually, each case carries a defensible policy rationale: competitive bidding, infrastructure modernisation, broader border-development guidelines, a sunrise storage sector, an unprecedented power-export agreement. Taken together, the picture is harder to dismiss. Across grain silos, airport privatisation, renewable energy land, cross-border power, and environmental approvals, a consistent mechanism repeats — a safeguard proposed by the government's own technical body was removed, and Adani Group was among the primary beneficiaries.

A fact-check of the five central claims finds four substantially accurate on the verifiable evidence. The anti-monopoly clause in the FCI silo programme was real, was dropped after NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs intervened in 2022, and a two-company duopoly — Adani Agri Logistics and Leap India — duly won 110 of 134 contracts worth over ₹16,500 crore. In the airport round, the DEA's recommended two-airport-per-bidder cap was not even discussed at the 11 December 2018 PPPAC meeting; the prior-experience requirement was removed; and Adani, with zero airport experience, won all six with bids that were, by any measure, aggressive — ₹177 per passenger at Ahmedabad against GMR's ₹85, ₹171 at Lucknow against AMP Capital's ₹139. At Khavda, defence border restrictions were relaxed in April–May 2023, SECI subsequently surrendered its 23,000-hectare parcel, and Adani Green expanded to roughly 19,000 hectares — the single largest allocation in the 30 GW park. At Godda, the plant remains the only one in India contracted to export 100 percent of its output to a neighbouring country, and it received SEZ status, Telegraph Act right-of-way powers, and a targeted August 2024 grid-connection amendment whose practical effect is confined to one project.

The one claim that does not hold at the same level of evidence is the Western Ghats environmental-clearance story. The Expert Appraisal Committee ultimately rejected and returned Adani's flagship expansion; anomalously fast final clearances are not what the record shows. What the record does show is a documented conflict of interest — an Adani adviser sitting on the appraisal committee — which is a serious institutional red flag, but a different one from the claim as originally framed.

On causation, the story must be honest about what is proven and what is inferred. Competitive-bidding defences are not mere deflection: Adani did bid highest on per-passenger fees, did win FCI contracts at lowest quoted rates, and is one of several developers at Khavda alongside NTPC, GSECL, and SECI. The government and Adani's counter-arguments are genuine, and each case individually carries a plausible non-conspiratorial explanation. What no single rebuttal addresses is the cumulative architecture: why, across five unrelated sectors over roughly a decade, the specific safeguard removed in each instance was the one whose removal most expanded the space available to a single group.

That is the institutional question this story ultimately poses — not about Adani alone, but about the design of India's PPP oversight architecture. PPPAC, the EAC, ministry override chains, sectoral PPP committees: each body operates with legitimate authority, each decision is procedurally defensible, and yet the NITI Aayog's own warning went unheeded at the decisive meeting. The Aayog had cautioned, in writing, that 'a bidder lacking sufficient technical capacity can well jeopardise the project.' Six airports later, that question remains open.

Regulatory capture is a systemic vulnerability, not merely a transaction. The pattern documented here — government technical bodies raising safeguards, those safeguards being overruled at higher levels, a single conglomerate collecting the gains across sectors — is real, documented, and demands institutional scrutiny regardless of which firm sits at the centre of it next time. Four of five claims: accurate on facts. Causation: contested. The architecture that makes the pattern possible: unchanged.
