## One Pattern, Five Sectors

Across five unrelated policy domains — airport privatisation, grain-silo contracts, renewable-energy land, a cross-border power plant, and environmental clearances — government technical bodies proposed safeguards before bidding opened. In at least four of those five domains, the proposals were overruled. In each case, the Adani Group was among the principal beneficiaries of what followed.

The safeguards took different forms: a cap on how many airports one bidder could win, an anti-monopoly clause in a food-storage tender, prior-experience requirements for airport operators. The bodies that raised them — the Department of Economic Affairs, NITI Aayog, the Food Corporation of India — were the government's own analytical arms. Their objections were recorded. They were set aside.

The result, across airports and silo contracts alone, was a concentration of public concessions and long-term agreements worth over ₹26,000 crore flowing to a single conglomerate. Whether that outcome reflects sound policy, regulatory failure, or something more deliberate is the question this investigation sets out to examine — sector by sector, document by document.

## One Pattern, Five Sectors

Across five years and five policy domains, a single structural sequence appears in the record: a government technical body proposes a safeguard — a cap, a clause, an eligibility requirement — it is overruled by a higher committee, and the Adani Group subsequently wins a disproportionate share of what is on offer. This investigation examines whether that pattern holds up to document-level scrutiny in airports, grain silos, renewable energy land, cross-border power, and environmental clearances. Four of the five claims prove substantially accurate on verifiable facts. The fifth is overstated.

In **airports** (2018–19), the Department of Economic Affairs recommended a two-airport cap per bidder and NITI Aayog flagged the risk of awarding contracts to an inexperienced operator. Both objections were overruled by the PPPAC and the Empowered Group of Secretaries. Prior airport-operation experience was dropped as a requirement; no cap was imposed. Adani Enterprises — with zero prior airport experience — bid the highest per-passenger fee across all six airports and won all six. Verdict: **Accurate**.

In **FCI grain silos**, FCI's own management proposed an anti-monopoly clause to prevent contract concentration. At a 2022 meeting, NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs objected, arguing market forces should prevail. The clause was dropped. Adani Agri Logistics and Leap India together won 110 of 134 contracts — roughly 78% of storage capacity — worth over ₹16,500 crore. Adani alone holds contracts exceeding ₹9,700 crore. Verdict: **Accurate**.

In **Khavda renewable energy land** (Kutch), defence border-construction restrictions were relaxed in April–May 2023. The state-run Solar Energy Corporation of India, which had held the largest parcel of 23,000 hectares in the park, subsequently surrendered that land in July 2023. Adani Green expanded to approximately 19,000 hectares — the single largest allocation in a multi-developer park that also includes NTPC, GSECL, GIPCL, SECI, and Suzlon. Verdict: **Accurate on the rule change and timeline; missing exclusivity context**.

In **Godda** (Jharkhand), the Adani power plant is the only facility in India contracted to export 100% of its output to a neighbouring country — in this case Bangladesh. It received SEZ status, Telegraph Act right-of-way powers for its dedicated transmission corridor, and a grid-connection amendment in August 2024 that, in practice, applies to no other operating facility. Verdict: **Accurate, with one factual correction** — the enabling export clause dates to the original December 2018 guidelines, not a later insertion as sometimes claimed.

In the **Western Ghats**, the picture is more complicated. The Maharashtra MoU (June 2022, ₹60,000 crore) and swift preliminary approvals are real. But the Expert Appraisal Committee ultimately rejected or returned Adani's flagship expansion and imposed additional scrutiny on all Western Ghats pumped-storage projects — the opposite of a fast-track outcome. The most serious documented irregularity is a conflict of interest: an Adani adviser sat on the appraisal committee. Verdict: **Overstated**.

Taken together, the five cases do not by themselves prove deliberate favouritism — each has a defensible policy rationale, and in several instances Adani prevailed in a genuinely competitive bid. What the documents do establish is a recurring upstream pattern: the government's own technical arms identified risks and proposed guardrails; those guardrails were removed before Adani entered the competition. Whether that pattern reflects policy capture or policy reform is the question this story puts to the evidence.

## Airports: The Cleanest Case

In the six-airport privatisation round of 2018–19 — covering Ahmedabad, Lucknow, Jaipur, Guwahati, Thiruvananthapuram, and Mangaluru — three conditions were set that together reshaped who could compete and how much they could win. Prior airport-operation experience was no longer required. No cap was placed on how many airports a single bidder could take. And the financial criterion shifted from revenue-sharing to a monthly per-passenger fee — the highest quote wins.

Both of the government's own technical arms raised formal objections before bidding opened. A Department of Economic Affairs note dated 10 December 2018 recommended that no more than two airports go to a single entity, arguing that spreading awards would enable 'yardstick competition' and contain capital risk. NITI Aayog questioned removing the experience requirement entirely, warning that a bidder 'lacking sufficient technical capacity can well jeopardise the project.' According to reporting citing the PPPAC meeting record, the DEA note was not discussed when the appraisal committee met the following day, 11 December 2018. The PPPAC ruled that prior airport experience 'may neither be made a prerequisite for bidding, nor a post-bid requirement.'

The outcome is a matter of public record. Adani Enterprises — with no previous airport-operation history — won all six concessions. Its bids were notably aggressive: for Ahmedabad, Adani quoted ₹177 per passenger against GMR's ₹85; for Lucknow, ₹171 against AMP Capital's ₹139. In 2020, Adani separately acquired Mumbai's Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport from the GVK Group, adding a seventh major airport and, with it, the forthcoming Navi Mumbai facility.

The government's account of events does not dispute the sequence but contests its significance. Civil Aviation Minister Hardeep Singh Puri told Parliament that the finance ministry and NITI Aayog were 'part of the decision-making process' and that the relaxations were designed 'to ensure higher competition and to give more flexibility to bidders.' Subhash Chandra Garg, who chaired the PPPAC, said: 'The decisions were taken by the PPPAC… I stand by those decisions, but the decisions were not mine alone.' Adani maintains no rules were violated, and credit-rating agency ICRA noted that the aggressive bids would deliver substantial annual revenue to Airports Authority of India.

What the primary documents establish — without requiring any inference — is that both the DEA's recommended two-airport ceiling and the experience criterion were specifically proposed as safeguards, both were recorded as objections from within government, and neither was adopted. The removal of the per-bidder cap is precisely the condition that made a clean sweep of all six airports possible for a single entrant.

## Silos and Solar: Where the Numbers Concentrate

In 2022, FCI's own engineers proposed an anti-monopoly clause for the Hub and Spoke silo modernisation programme — a roughly ₹20,000 crore effort to replace conventional grain godowns with modern steel silos under public-private partnership. The clause would have stopped any single company from cornering contracts across the 134-tender programme. At a 2022 appraisal meeting, NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs pushed back, arguing to 'let market forces prevail.' The clause was dropped.

The outcome was striking. Adani Agri Logistics and its associate Leap India Food & Logistics together won 110 of the 134 Phase I contracts — worth more than ₹16,500 crore — and secured storage rights over roughly 46.5 lakh metric tonnes (LMT) out of a total 60 LMT tendered. The All India Kisan Sabha put the concentration figure at 'nearly 77.5%' of capacity. Adani alone holds contracts valued at over ₹9,700 crore.

The government and FCI offer a direct rebuttal: awards went to the lowest bidders in a process with 3 to 15 competing firms per project, and in Phase II Adani won nothing at all — a result FCI says 'demonstrated that there was no preferential treatment.' FCI's official statement called silos a 'sunrise sector' where restricting bidding 'could have reduced competition.' The Phase II outcome is a genuine complication for the strongest version of the favouritism narrative.

The surviving critic argument is structural rather than procedural: removing an anti-monopoly safeguard predictably enables concentration regardless of whether individual bids are contested. That removal was driven not by private lobbying that can be documented, but by the government's own technical bodies — the same recurring pattern seen in the airports case.

Five hundred kilometres west, a different kind of concentration is visible from satellite imagery. The Khavda Renewable Energy Park in the Rann of Kutch — a flat, near-uninhabited salt desert on the India–Pakistan border — is being developed as the world's largest single-location renewable energy zone, targeting 30 GW across roughly 72,600 hectares.

The land near the border had long been subject to defence construction restrictions. On 21 April 2023, defence-ministry officials met with the Army, the renewable-energy ministry, and Gujarat state officials. Consensus decisions allowed wind turbines as close as 1–2 km from the border and hybrid plants from 2–8 km. New guidelines circulated on 8 May 2023 formalised the relaxation, extending it to borders with Pakistan, China, Bangladesh, Nepal, and Myanmar. The Guardian reported that the BJP 'lobbied at the highest levels for the protocols to be relaxed to make land in the Rann of Kutch available for both solar and wind construction.'

The land reallocation that followed has attracted the most scrutiny. SECI — the central government's own renewable energy arm — had been allocated the largest single parcel at 23,000 hectares. Despite attending the April 2023 meeting and receiving the benefit of the relaxation, SECI wrote to Gujarat in July 2023 seeking to surrender its allocation. Gujarat took the land back. Adani Green Energy subsequently expanded its holding in the park to approximately 19,000 hectares, representing roughly 9.5 GW of planned capacity — the largest single-developer share.

Khavda is not, however, an Adani-exclusive project. NTPC holds capacity of approximately 4,750 MW; Gujarat State Electricity Corporation, Gujarat Industries Power Company, SECI itself, and Suzlon's Sarjan Realties are also allocated parcels. The government frames the defence relaxation as a general infrastructure policy applying to all borders and all developers equally.

The question that neither the government rebuttal nor the available documents resolve is the one that sits at the centre of the Khavda case: why did a state-owned enterprise surrender 23,000 hectares of newly valuable land — land made significantly more valuable by a rule change it had itself benefited from — and why did that land subsequently expand Adani's position rather than return to the open allocation pool? That question remains open.

## Godda: One Plant, One Customer

In the coal-rich district of Godda, Jharkhand, stands a power plant with no precedent in Indian energy policy. Every unit of electricity generated at Adani Power's 1,600-MW facility is contracted to cross an international border — destined entirely for Bangladesh under a long-term agreement with state utility BPDB. No other Indian generating station has ever been licensed to export 100% of its output to a neighbouring country.

A widely repeated version of this story holds that the enabling clause — permitting a dedicated cross-border transmission line for an exclusively export-oriented plant — was quietly inserted into Ministry of Power guidelines in July 2019, after Adani had already won the Bangladesh contract. Primary-document verification does not support that timeline. The original Guidelines for Import/Export (Cross Border) of Electricity, issued on 18 December 2018 under MoP Office Memorandum No. 14/1/2017-Trans, already contained clause 8.9, explicitly permitting 'Indian Generating Stations supplying electricity exclusively to neighbouring country' to build a dedicated transmission line. A 3 July 2019 addendum changed only clause 5.2(a) — adding gas as a permitted fuel category — and stated plainly that 'other contents… remain unchanged.' Clause 8.9 was untouched.

The consequential later amendment came on 12 August 2024, when a fresh MoP office memorandum added a proviso to clause 8.9 allowing the government to 'permit co-use' of the dedicated transmission line. In theory this applies to any future cross-border generator. In practice, Godda is currently the only plant the clause can affect — making it a structural change with a single real-world beneficiary.

The structural anomalies that are fully documented sit elsewhere. Godda was granted Special Economic Zone status — an industrial classification typically reserved for export-oriented manufacturing clusters, not power stations — which conferred tax advantages and exemptions unavailable to any conventional generator. The dedicated 400-kV transmission line connecting Godda to the Bangladesh border was also granted Telegraph Act right-of-way powers, giving Adani acquisition authority over the land corridor that would ordinarily require separate negotiation or state intervention. No other Indian power producer holds an equivalent combination of privileges for a cross-border arrangement.

The government's position is that each of these designations was enabled by existing statute, applied as written, and that the SEZ classification reflects Godda's genuinely export-oriented character under the legal definition. Critics counter that no policy framework for cross-border-only generation existed in India before Godda — the rules were, in effect, written around a project already under construction. Whether that sequence reflects responsive regulation or regulatory accommodation is the question the documents leave open.

## Western Ghats: Where the Claim Breaks Down

In June 2022, Maharashtra signed a memorandum of understanding with Adani for investments of ₹60,000 crore, covering projects across the ecologically sensitive Western Ghats. Preliminary environmental appraisals moved quickly, and critics framed the pace as evidence of a clearance process tilted in Adani's favour. On the surface, the pattern looked familiar — another sector, another set of rules bending early.

But the final decisions tell a different story. The Expert Appraisal Committee — the statutory body that issues environmental clearances — ultimately rejected and returned Adani's flagship Western Ghats mining expansion, one of the most significant projects in the pipeline. The EAC also applied heightened scrutiny to all pumped-storage hydropower projects proposed in the Western Ghats, not only those linked to Adani. The pattern of anomalously fast final clearances that the claim required simply does not appear in the record of concluded decisions.

That makes this the one case in the investigation rated overstated. Fast preliminary appraisals and a high-value MoU are real and documented. What is not supported is the stronger charge — that the environmental clearance machinery delivered accelerated final approvals as a matter of course. Where the process was tested most seriously, it pushed back.

There is, however, a genuine and distinct red flag in the record. During the initial review stage, an adviser with a documented relationship to the Adani Group sat on the EAC appraisal committee. A conflict of interest of that kind is a serious procedural concern — it goes to the integrity of the deliberative process regardless of what the committee ultimately decided. The concern is real; it is simply a different allegation from fast-tracked clearances, and conflating the two weakens both.

The evidentiary standard applied across this investigation requires distinguishing between what the documents show and what they are being used to claim. In the Western Ghats case, the documented conflict of interest deserves scrutiny on its own terms. The clearance-speed claim, as stated, is not borne out.

## What the Evidence Does — and Doesn't — Show

Across five sectors and roughly five years, a structural thread recurs. In airports, the Department of Economic Affairs and NITI Aayog recommended a two-airport cap and a prior-experience requirement; both were overruled by the PPPAC and the Empowered Group of Secretaries before bidding opened. In grain silos, FCI itself proposed an anti-monopoly clause; it was dropped in 2022 after NITI Aayog and DEA intervened. At Khavda, defence-border restrictions were relaxed in April–May 2023, SECI then surrendered its 23,000-hectare parcel in July, and Adani expanded into the freed land. At Godda, a plant contracted to export 100% of its output to Bangladesh received SEZ status, Telegraph Act right-of-way powers, and a grid-connection amendment in August 2024 that in practice applies to no other facility in India. In each case, the government's own technical watchdog arms proposed a safeguard; the safeguard was removed; Adani was among the direct beneficiaries.

The material benefit in each case is documented in the public record. Adani Agri Logistics holds silo contracts worth over ₹9,700 crore; together with Leap India, the two companies took 110 of 134 awards — roughly 78% of contracted storage capacity — in a programme worth more than ₹16,500 crore. In the 2018–19 airport round, Adani, then holding zero airport-operation experience, won all six concessions with bids that outpaced rivals by wide margins — ₹177 per passenger against GMR's ₹85 at Ahmedabad, ₹171 against AMP Capital's ₹139 at Lucknow. At Khavda, Adani holds approximately 19,000 hectares, representing around 9.5 GW of capacity — the single largest allocation in a park that also includes NTPC, GSECL, GIPCL, SECI and Suzlon. Godda remains the only Indian generating station contracted to send 100% of its output abroad.

What the evidence does not establish is proof of coordination or deliberate instruction to benefit Adani specifically. The government's rebuttal in each case is not without substance. Airport bids were genuinely competitive — Adani bid the most aggressively and won on those terms; removing the experience requirement did, as ministers argued, widen the eligible pool. In silos, between three and fifteen firms competed per project, and Adani won nothing in Phase II. Khavda is a multi-developer park with large public-sector parcels; the border relaxation extended to all frontiers — Pakistan, China, Bangladesh, Nepal, Myanmar — and applied to all developers, not Adani alone. Godda's export arrangement and transmission rights were written into the original December 2018 cross-border guidelines, not inserted later.

The contest, then, is not over the underlying facts — those are substantially accurate in four of the five cases — but over the causal step. Were safeguards removed because of a genuine policy shift toward competition, infrastructure urgency, and border development? Or were those rationales post-hoc cover for decisions whose structural effect was predictable? That question cannot be resolved from the documents alone. What the documents do establish is the recurring signature: the government's own expert arms identified the risks, proposed limits, and were overruled — repeatedly, across unrelated sectors, within a compressed window.

One question sits at the centre of the Khavda case and remains openly unresolved. SECI attended the April 2023 meeting where border relaxations were agreed, received the benefit of those relaxations alongside every other developer, and then three months later wrote to surrender its 23,000-hectare parcel — the largest single allocation in the park — back to Gujarat. That land was subsequently taken up by Adani. No public document explains why a state-backed energy corporation voluntarily relinquished viable, newly de-restricted renewable land. Until that explanation is on record, the Khavda sequence cannot be read as routine reallocation.

Whether the pattern across airports, silos, Khavda, Godda, and the Western Ghats constitutes regulatory capture is a conclusion this investigation does not make for you. The structural finding — that safeguards proposed by the government's own bodies were removed before Adani bid, and that Adani benefited materially in four of the five cases — is documented. The inference about intent and causation is yours to draw.
