# Airtel's ₹2.1 Trillion Year

For the financial year ended March 2026, Bharti Airtel crossed ₹2.1 trillion in consolidated revenue — a 16% jump from the year before. Behind that number: 373 million subscribers paying more, consuming more data, and generating free cash flows that are systematically dismantling the company's debt pile. This is not a story of disruption. It is a story of a capital-heavy business finally achieving the scale to print money.

---

## ₹2.1 Trillion

Full-year consolidated revenue. Up 16% from ₹1.82 trillion in FY25, and 28% from ₹1.64 trillion in FY24. Airtel is now compounding revenue in the double digits — rare for a telecom at this scale, remarkable for an Indian company operating in what was once a free-calling, near-zero-ARPU market.

## Five Quarters of Acceleration

Quarter after quarter, Airtel's revenue has pointed in one direction. From ₹478.8 billion in Q4 FY25 to ₹553.8 billion in Q4 FY26, the company added ₹75 billion in quarterly run-rate in a single year. The engine: a July 2024 tariff hike that lifted average revenue per user by roughly 20%, followed by steady subscriber net-adds that absorbed the higher pricing without meaningful churn. Monthly churn stayed at 2.4% — a signal that Airtel's subscribers had nowhere better to go.

## 57.8% EBITDA Margin

Q4 FY26 EBITDA margin — up from 57.2% a year ago. Annual EBITDA crossed ₹1.2 trillion for the first time. One of the highest margins in global telecom, achieved not by cutting costs but by spreading fixed infrastructure costs across a growing revenue base.

## Margins Built to Last

This is what operating leverage looks like in telecom: a fixed-cost network of 346,000 towers and 532,000 km of fibre, spread across more revenue every quarter. EBIT for Q4 FY26 grew 21% year-on-year — faster than revenue, faster than EBITDA. EBIT margin expanded to 32.8%. Every incremental rupee of top-line is dropping disproportionately to the bottom. The infrastructure advantage that Airtel spent two decades building is now printing money.

## ₹257 ARPU

India mobile average revenue per user, Q4 FY26. Up 5% from ₹245 a year ago. When Airtel raised tariffs in July 2024, analysts feared mass defection to Jio. Monthly churn stayed at 2.4%. The subscribers paid up and kept paying. Postpaid users — higher-ARPU, lower-churn — jumped 74.6% year-on-year to 106 million.

## The Data Explosion

The data story is more explosive than the ARPU story. Average monthly data consumption per user grew 25% year-on-year to 31.4 GB. Total data traffic surged 32.8% to 27,985 million GBs in Q4. And 80.1% of Airtel's mobile base is now on 4G or 5G — up from 77.8% a year ago. Airtel has 1.19 million broadband base stations monetizing every GB scrolled, streamed, and downloaded.

## +41.7% Homes Subscribers

Year-on-year growth in Airtel broadband home customers. The company ended March 2026 with 14.2 million home subscribers — up from 10 million a year ago. This is not a rounding error. Airtel added 4.2 million home broadband customers in a single year, 1.1 million in Q4 alone.

## Homes: The Quiet Juggernaut

Home ARPU at ₹527 per month — more than double the mobile ARPU. OTT consumption, work-from-home habits, school-age children needing reliable connectivity: India's home internet demand is a secular shift that Airtel is positioned to capture. The capex is front-loaded (₹68 billion in FY26 for homes), but each subscriber that connects locks in ₹527 per month at margins that will expand as the network amortizes.

## +22% Africa Revenue

Airtel Africa's constant-currency revenue growth in Q4 FY26. The full-year growth rate: 24% in constant currency across 14 countries and 166 million subscribers. After years of currency headwinds masking operational progress, the underlying business is in acceleration mode.

## Africa Turns the Corner

EBITDA margin expanded 2.2 percentage points to 50.4% — the operating leverage is inflecting. The Africa business generated $516 million in operating free cash flow for the year, up 30% in constant currency. The bear case was that Africa would permanently drag on group returns. That narrative is over. Nigeria, Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda: these are now profitable growth markets.

## Airtel Money: Africa's Hidden Compounding Engine

Airtel Money — mobile payments across all 14 African countries — processed $49 billion in transactions in Q4 FY26 alone, up 34% year-on-year. Monthly active users grew 21.3% to 54.1 million. Revenue grew 25.7% in constant currency. In markets where traditional banking is inaccessible to most of the population, Airtel Money is not competing with bank apps — it is the bank. The fintech optionality in the Africa business is systematically underpriced.

## -19% Net Debt

Net debt fell from ₹2,038 billion at March 2025 to ₹1,648 billion at March 2026. That is ₹390 billion of debt destroyed in 12 months. The mechanism: generate ₹1.2 trillion in EBITDA, spend ₹475 billion in capex, use the remaining ₹737 billion in operating free cash flow — up 18% from FY25 — to service interest and retire borrowings.

## Demolishing the Debt

Three years ago, Airtel's leverage was the primary bear case. Debt-to-EBITDA (excluding leases) was at levels that made credit analysts nervous. Today, operating free cash flow is growing faster than capex, the debt pile is shrinking, and the company's interest coverage ratio is structurally improving. The balance sheet transformation is happening in real time.

## +51% Net Income

Full-year net income growth, before exceptional items. Annual net income reached ₹269 billion in FY26, up from ₹177.6 billion in FY25 and ₹116.2 billion in FY24. In three years, Airtel has more than doubled shareholder earnings. Q4 FY26 alone delivered ₹72.4 billion in net income, up 39% year-on-year.

## The Profit Surge

The operating leverage is now cascading all the way down the income statement. Revenue grew 16%. EBITDA grew 15.5%. EBIT grew 20%. Profit before tax grew 31% for the year and 36% in Q4 alone. Finance costs fell 2% as the debt load shrank. The cascade effect of operating leverage plus deleveraging is driving exponential profit growth from linear revenue growth.

## ₹1,784

Airtel's closing share price on March 31, 2026. Market capitalization: $132 billion. Enterprise value: ₹13,222 billion. EV/EBITDA: 9.77x. Price-to-earnings: 38.83x. The stock returned +25.1% over the year — outperforming the Nifty 50 by 30 percentage points.

## What the Market Sees in Airtel

The market is paying a premium because it is pricing in what comes next: the homes business scaling to 20–30 million subscribers, Africa margins crossing 55%, and the continued debt paydown that multiplies free cash per share each year. The Sunil Mittal thesis — premium positioning, network investment, emerging-market optionality — is working. The compounding machine is running.
