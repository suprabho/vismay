## A School in Minab: The Sharpest Edge of AI War

On a spring morning in 2026, a missile strike obliterated an elementary school in Minab, Iran. At least 175 people were killed — the majority of them children. The strike was part of a broader US bombing campaign in which AI targeting systems, including Palantir's Maven Smart System and Anthropic's Claude, were widely used. Whether those systems directly generated the fatal targeting call remains contested. What is not contested is the body count.

The Minab school strike is the sharpest, most visceral illustration yet of what happens when artificial intelligence enters the kill chain. It is also a warning: in a future conflict between nuclear-armed superpowers, errors of this magnitude would not end with a diplomatic crisis. They could end with a war.

## From Ukraine to Gaza to Iran: AI Goes to War

Battlefield AI is no longer a future threat — it is a present one, operating across three active or recent theatres simultaneously. Palantir's Maven Smart System, the same platform integrated with drone feeds and satellite imagery, passes dozens of potential Russian targets per day to Ukrainian commanders. Kyiv has supplemented that with its own homegrown coordination tool, nicknamed 'Uber for artillery,' which routes fire orders across a fluid frontline faster than any human staff officer could manage.

In Gaza, the Israeli military's 'Lavender' system applied machine learning to flag Hamas targets for strike approval. Reports from investigative journalists suggest the system carried an error rate of roughly 10 percent — a figure that, scaled across hundreds of strikes, translates into a significant number of misidentified individuals.

The most consequential use — and the hardest to fully account for — came during the US bombing campaign in Iran. Both Claude and Maven Smart System were widely used throughout that campaign, according to US officials. Whether AI target-selection contributed directly to the missile strike that killed at least 175 people at a school in Minab — most of them children — remains unconfirmed, but the question itself signals how far and how fast these systems have moved from the testing range to the kill chain.

## The Escalation Ladder: From Cold War Theory to AI Reality

In 1965, RAND Corporation strategist Herman Kahn published a model that would define Cold War crisis management for decades: a 44-rung escalation ladder, ascending from a 'nonviolent cold war' at the bottom to an apocalyptic nuclear 'spasm' at rung 44. Kahn's framework — cold in its rationality, and an acknowledged inspiration for Stanley Kubrick's Dr. Strangelove — was designed to map the deliberate, step-by-step logic of nuclear brinkmanship, preserving the pauses in which human leaders could de-escalate before catastrophe.

The central fear of the nuclear era was never that a rational actor would choose the top of the ladder. It was that miscalculation, miscommunication, or technical failure would skip rungs involuntarily. The Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962 made that fear viscerally real: the captain of Soviet submarine B-59, cut off from Moscow after being struck by US depth charges, came within one officer's veto of firing a nuclear-armed torpedo. The crisis ultimately produced the Washington-Moscow hotline — a literal mechanism for slowing the climb.

AI-enabled battlefield systems now threaten to compress that ladder in ways Kahn never modelled. Where Cold War escalation unfolded over hours or days — giving diplomats and commanders time to signal, interpret, and respond — an AI naval defense system can detect a threat and recommend a strike faster than a human can review the order. In the Hoover Institution's wargame simulation, a Chinese AI hub malfunctioning and firing on a US vessel required no human decision at all. The rungs weren't climbed; they were skipped.

The implications for nuclear-armed rivals like the US and China are profound. A conventional exchange that triggers automated responses on both sides could reach a nuclear threshold before any leader has formally authorised escalation. The question Kahn's framework was built to answer — 'how do we signal our position on the ladder?' — may have no answer when the systems doing the signalling operate in milliseconds.
