# A Rocket Company That Became the Internet

SpaceX's S-1 reveals three companies inside one stock — and only one of them makes money.

## $18.7B headline

SpaceX consolidated revenue, full year 2025.

The headline number is $18.7 billion — but it hides more than it reveals. Revenue grew 33% year-on-year from $14.1 billion in 2024, accelerating into Q1 2026 at a run rate approaching $18.8 billion annualised. The growth story is real. What is doing the growing, who is paying for it, and what it costs are three entirely different questions.

## Three segments, three stories

Pull the segments apart and the consolidated P&L becomes a story about one profitable business keeping two unprofitable ones alive.

The **Connectivity segment** — Starlink, the satellite internet network — generated $11.4 billion in revenue in 2025, grew 49.8% year-on-year, posted $4.4 billion in operating income, and produced $7.2 billion in Adjusted EBITDA on a margin approaching 63%. For a capital-intensive infrastructure business deploying satellites at scale, that margin is remarkable.

The **Space segment** — Falcon launches, Dragon crew missions, Starship development — generated $4.1 billion in revenue, recorded a $657 million operating loss, and burned $3 billion on Starship R&D alone. The rocket business generates less revenue in a full year than Starlink earns in a single quarter.

The **AI segment** — xAI including Grok and X, acquired February 2026 — generated $3.2 billion in revenue but absorbed $6.4 billion in operating losses and $12.7 billion in capital expenditure across 2025. These financials were added to the S-1 through a retroactive common-control recast, which means the 2025 numbers look worse than the standalone SpaceX they once were.

## Starlink: the machine behind the curtain

To understand SpaceX's finances, understand first that Starlink is not a feature. It is the business.

As of 31 March 2026, Starlink counted 10.3 million subscribers across 164 countries, territories, and markets — up from 5.0 million a year earlier, a 106% doubling in twelve months. The network runs approximately 9,600 satellites in Low-Earth Orbit. A dedicated satellite-to-mobile constellation of 650 V1 Mobile satellites reaches 7.4 million monthly unique devices across approximately 30 countries.

In 2024, Starlink revenue was approximately $7.7 billion. In 2025 it reached $11.4 billion — 83% growth in a single year on a constellation that was already the largest LEO broadband network in history. What makes the trajectory extraordinary is the margin alongside it: 63% Adjusted EBITDA on $11.4 billion in revenue. At that scale, the marginal cost of adding a subscriber approaches near-zero on infrastructure already in orbit.

The next phase is already loaded. V3 satellites — each carrying one terabit per second of downlink capacity — are scheduled to begin deployment on Starship in the second half of 2026. A single Starship launch carries up to 60 V3 satellites versus three V2 Mini satellites on a Falcon 9. That is a twentyfold capacity increase per launch, arriving just as subscriber growth accelerates.

Consumer broadband gets the attention. The larger opportunity is elsewhere. Maritime ARPU runs approximately $34,000 per year. Aviation ARPU runs approximately $300,000. SpaceX holds less than 1% market share in both segments today.

## 10.3M subscribers

Starlink subscribers as of March 31, 2026 · 164 countries · +106% year-on-year.

Subscribers doubled in each of the last two years: 2.3 million at end-2023, 4.6 million at end-2024, 9.2 million by end-2025, and 10.3 million by Q1 2026. Quilty Space projects Starlink revenues reach roughly $20 billion in 2026 — approximately 79% of SpaceX's total projected revenue for the year, before AI and Space segments are counted.

Average revenue per user (ARPU) fell 18% year-on-year to $81 per month. The trade-off is straightforward: more subscribers at lower price points, with enterprise, maritime, and aviation contracts pulling the blended revenue figure up from below.

## Space: the rocket that ate the budget

The Space segment produced $4.1 billion in revenue in 2025. It also spent $3 billion on Starship, a vehicle that has not yet delivered a commercial payload to orbit.

The cumulative Starship R&D spend disclosed in the S-1 exceeds $15 billion — more than the programme's original budget. SpaceX expects Starship to begin commercial payload delivery in the second half of 2026. In Q1 2026, the Space segment generated only $619 million in revenue against $662 million in operating losses, with $930 million in Starship R&D funded through the quarter.

The launch business is currently a net drain on the balance sheet — by design. Falcon 9 maintains a 99%+ mission success rate and carries more than 80% of mass to orbit globally. Booster reuse is now routine, with flight-proven boosters completing 20+ missions. Launch costs that historically ran into the billions per mission now run in the tens of millions. The paradox: this engineering excellence serves as Starlink's supply chain, not Space's cash generator. Falcon launches are how Starlink satellites reach orbit. As Starship becomes operational, V3 constellation deployment becomes cheaper per bit delivered — closing the loop back into Connectivity margin expansion.

The question for investors is whether Starship transforms this segment from a cost centre into a second profit engine by 2027–2028, or whether it remains the company's most expensive science project.

## $15B+ on Starship

Cumulative Starship R&D investment per S-1 disclosure — more than the original programme budget.

SpaceX spent $3 billion on Starship in 2025 alone, and $930 million in Q1 2026. The vehicle has not yet delivered a commercial payload to orbit. Every other item on the long-term roadmap — V3 satellite deployment, orbital AI compute, lunar operations, Mars — runs through it.

SpaceX has publicly described Starship as the key enabler of its entire long-term growth strategy. The logic is internally consistent: Starship's payload capacity to LEO — potentially 100–150 metric tons on a fully reusable basis — is the physical prerequisite for V3 satellite deployment, orbital AI compute infrastructure, lunar operations, and ultimately Mars. No Starship, no roadmap. The $15 billion is the option premium on everything that follows.

## The AI black hole

The xAI acquisition, closed 2 February 2026, was presented as a strategic union of SpaceX's launch and connectivity capabilities with frontier AI. In financial terms, it imported a $6.4 billion operating loss and $12.7 billion in capital expenditure into the consolidated statement retroactively.

The AI segment's 2025 financials show $3.2 billion in revenue against $6.4 billion in operating losses — a negative operating margin of nearly 200%. In Q1 2026 alone, the segment burned $7.7 billion in capex against $818 million in revenue.

The COLOSSUS data centre in Memphis, Tennessee — described as the world's first coherent gigawatt-scale AI training cluster — houses 220,000 Nvidia GPUs across 300MW of power, built in 120 days. A major Anthropic contract contributes $1.25 billion per month in compute revenue through May 2029. Traders should note that either party can terminate the contract with 90 days' notice. It is a large revocable purchase order, not recurring SaaS revenue.

Grok had 117 million MAUs using its AI features as of March 2026, within a combined X and Grok ecosystem of 550 million monthly actives. The strategic logic — Grok integrated with X's real-time data outperforms static-training models — is plausible. The financial logic requires Grok to reach enterprise revenue scale significantly faster than the current burn rate suggests.

SpaceX intends to begin deploying orbital AI compute satellites as early as 2028 — data centres in Sun-synchronous orbit, powered by solar, cooled passively through radiation into space. SpaceX can launch these cheaper than anyone else. Whether the workloads exist at scale to fill them is a different question.

## The EBITDA bridge

SpaceX reported Adjusted EBITDA of $6.6 billion in 2025 alongside a GAAP net loss of $4.9 billion. In Q1 2026, Adjusted EBITDA of $1.1 billion coexisted with a GAAP net loss of $4.3 billion.

The distance between those numbers is bridged by satellite constellation depreciation, stock-based compensation, and interest expense on $29.1 billion in long-term debt. These are real cash costs even when classified non-cash on the income statement. The accumulated deficit stands at $41.3 billion. The company's Adjusted EBITDA is doing significant narrative work: it correctly reflects the genuine strength of the Connectivity segment while obscuring the weight of the AI segment's burn. Both things are simultaneously true.

In 2024, standalone SpaceX (before the xAI recast) generated approximately $791 million in net income. The xAI merger turned a profitable company into a loss-maker. Whether that is a red flag or a strategic choice is the defining question of the IPO.

## Capital at the speed of ambition

SpaceX's 2025 capital expenditure was approximately $20.7 billion: $3.8 billion in Space, $4.2 billion in Connectivity, and $12.7 billion in AI. In Q1 2026, the AI segment alone spent $7.7 billion in capex — the equivalent of most technology companies' full annual investment budget, in a single quarter.

To contextualise the scale: the global space economy was $613 billion in 2024 per the Space Foundation. The total addressable broadband market encompasses over 3 billion unconnected people. The global AI compute market is, across any reasonable forecast, in the tens of trillions over the next decade. SpaceX is investing against all three simultaneously. The question is whether it can convert capital at this velocity into durable cash flow before the debt structure requires a different conversation.

The SpaceX Bridge Loan (Goldman Sachs, March 2026) and the SpaceX Credit Facility (Bank of America, February 2025, extended and upsized May 2026) frame the balance sheet context. $29.1 billion in long-term debt at March 2026 is not trivial against a business still generating GAAP losses.

## Elon's kingdom

SpaceX lists Class A common stock on Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX. Class A shares carry one vote each. Class B shares carry ten votes each. Elon Musk holds his interests primarily through Class B. Following the IPO, Musk will hold approximately 42% of the economic equity but control approximately 85% of the voting power.

SpaceX will qualify as a "controlled company" under Nasdaq corporate governance rules and has stated its intention to rely on exemptions from certain requirements — including those related to independent board composition and compensation committee independence.

The governance structure is that of a founder-controlled empire. For investors, the question is not whether Musk's judgment on SpaceX specifically has historically been sound — it has been extraordinary — but whether the same concentrated control that enabled rapid decision-making also insulates the company from governance pressures that public markets typically impose. Musk is simultaneously running Tesla, leading xAI, advising the U.S. government through DOGE, and pursuing Mars colonisation. The S-1 acknowledges this directly in its risk factors.

## $1.75T at IPO

Implied IPO valuation per analyst estimates — price range not set in S-1.

SpaceX would list among Apple, Microsoft, and Nvidia at this valuation — the only company in history to reach the $1.75 trillion threshold on negative trailing net income. The price is not set in the S-1; it will be determined during the June 2026 roadshow.

At that valuation, the implied multiple prices the Connectivity segment at roughly 90× its 2025 operating income — expensive for satellite infrastructure, defensible for a broadband near-monopoly across 164 markets with no terrestrial competition in most of them. The Space segment is valued at its option on Starship and the lunar economy, not current cash generation. The AI segment is valued on a belief that Grok, X, and orbital compute compound into something that justifies $12.7 billion in annual capex.

The valuation trajectory tells its own story: $12 billion in 2015, $100 billion in 2021, $800 billion at the December 2025 tender offer, $1.25 trillion at the xAI merger in February 2026, $1.75 trillion at IPO target. It doubled in six months before the roadshow began.

Whether any individual investor should own SPCX at that price depends on which of the three companies they believe in, at what timeline, and how they price governance risk in a structure where a single man controls 85% of the votes. The S-1 is the most transparent SpaceX has ever been. What it reveals is a company of extraordinary engineering capability, extraordinary ambition, and a balance sheet that requires extraordinary patience.

## When class wore the mask of margin

The cleanest lens for understanding the SpaceX S-1 is this: Starlink is funding a portfolio of bets that no conventional capital structure would be willing to hold simultaneously.

Starlink's $7.2 billion in 2025 Adjusted EBITDA covers the $3 billion Starship R&D spend with $4 billion to spare. It partially offsets the AI segment's operating losses. It services the debt. It provides the operating leverage that allows management to present Adjusted EBITDA of $6.6 billion as the company's "true" earnings power — while the GAAP statement shows a $4.9 billion loss.

The clean binary of "profitable Starlink, unprofitable everything else" was always an approximation of a messier truth. A 90-day cancellable Anthropic contract is a swing factor in AI segment revenue. Starship's commercial arrival — repeatedly delayed, now targeted for H2 2026 — determines whether the Space segment's losses converge or compound. Grok's ability to monetise beyond API revenue determines whether the xAI acquisition was strategic foresight or an expensive merger of convenience ahead of a liquidity event.

Investors buying SPCX are not buying a company. They are buying a thesis about the architecture of the next fifty years.
