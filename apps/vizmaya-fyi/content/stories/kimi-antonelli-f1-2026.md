# Four poles. Three wins. Twenty grid spots lost.

*A nineteen-year-old from Bologna is leading a Formula 1 World Championship that nobody, four months ago, expected him to be leading. The car helps. The starts do not.*

**By vizmaya · May 2026**

---

## The Brackley winter

The Antonelli that walked into the Mercedes factory in January 2026 was not the same Antonelli who had melted down in qualifying at Mexico City eight weeks earlier. There was a meeting — Toto Wolff, his father Marco, Kimi — that he would later describe as a hard reset. There was work with a sports psychologist in Bologna through the off-season. There was swimming, every morning, for cardio that didn't punish a back tired from a year of being thrown around an F1 cockpit.

When the Mercedes-AMG F1 W17 E PERFORMANCE was unveiled on 22 January, the entire grid had a problem and Antonelli alone had an opportunity. The 2026 regulation reset wiped out everything any active driver knew about how to extract pace from a Formula 1 car. The wheelbase was 200 mm shorter. The bodywork was 100 mm narrower. The minimum weight dropped by 30 kg. The DRS flap had been replaced by active aerodynamics on both ends of the car. The MGU-H was gone. Guenther Steiner, watching from outside, said the quiet part out loud: "Kimi has no baggage from previous generations of cars. He has no old habits to unlearn, while George has to adapt."

That, on paper, is why a sophomore was suddenly the Mercedes ace.

## 350

*kilowatts of MGU-K. Nearly triple the unit it replaced.*

The number that matters most on the W17 is the one that lives under the rear axle. The MGU-K's electrical output jumped from 120 kW to 350 kW, the MGU-H was abolished, and the energy split between the internal combustion engine and the battery is now close to fifty-fifty. The total weight saving and the active wings mean the W17 is the fastest thing in a straight line in 2026, and by a long way — telemetry from the Albert Park main straight had Russell's car peaking around 334 km/h to Leclerc's Ferrari around 285 km/h on a comparable deployment. The car is a missile. The launch sequence to set the missile in motion is the most fragile thing on the grid.

## Albert Park, Q3

The weekend opened badly. Antonelli destroyed his car in FP3 and triggered the red flag. He stitched together a recovery in qualifying that nobody on the pit wall thought was possible after the morning's accident — a 1:18.811, just 0.293 s shy of Russell's pole. Front row, but unmistakably the wingman.

Verstappen, three months on from publicly debating retirement, had crashed in Q1 and would start P20. That, more than anyone wanted to admit on Saturday night, was the new normal.

## P2 → P7

*by Turn 3, with the battery in the dirt.*

What Antonelli's dash flagged at lights-out was battery starvation. The W17's 2026 launch sequence requires the driver to hold high revs for at least ten seconds before the green light to spool the turbo — the MGU-H is gone, and there is no longer an exhaust path keeping the boost alive on idle. Get it wrong and the electric deployment drops a state-of-charge band the instant the clutch bites. Antonelli got it wrong. He dropped from P2 to P7 by the exit of Turn 3 as Leclerc surged from P4 to lead and the Ferraris squeezed Russell into a sandwich.

It was the first time in 2026 that a Ferrari got off the line better than the Mercedes in front of it. It was not the last.

## Hadjar's smoke, Bottas's tow

The race was won, in the end, by a Red Bull-Ford engine failure. Isack Hadjar, on debut weekend for the new Red Bull Powertrains unit, parked his RB22 trailing smoke on Lap 11. The Virtual Safety Car came out. Mercedes pitted both cars under neutralisation. Ferrari elected to stay out, and Hamilton said everything that needed to be said on the radio afterward: "One of us should have come in."

When the second VSC came on Lap 16 for a stricken Bottas, the pit lane was closed for car recovery. Ferrari was stranded. Leclerc and Hamilton had to make a normal-condition stop later and emerged behind both Mercedes. Russell crossed the line for victory. Antonelli was +2.974 s back, having clawed the gap from six seconds in the closing laps on hards that were forty-six laps old. He told Sky Sports F1 afterward: "It was not the best start we could have wished for. I lost a lot of places and I had to recover. But overall it was a good race, the pace was really strong, especially at the end."

The W17 was the class of the field. The start was the problem. The pattern was set.

## 19y 6m 18d

*Youngest pole-sitter in Formula 1 history.*

Shanghai was supposed to be Russell's. In the closing minutes of Q3 his car stuck itself in first gear and he limped it back to the pits with a powertrain warning lighting up the dash. Antonelli, calm enough in the second car to be irritating, opened with a 1:32.322 and improved on the second push to a 1:32.064. Russell rebuilt the car on the gantry, took one effort on a single out-lap, set a 1:32.286, and locked out the front row anyway. The intra-Mercedes margin was 0.222 s — and the record was Antonelli's.

The mark he broke had stood for eighteen years. Sebastian Vettel had taken pole at Monza in 2008 for Toro Rosso at 21 years 72 days. Antonelli is now the youngest pole-sitter in the sport's history by more than eighteen months. Hamilton, from P3 in a Ferrari: "A great record. It's going to take a while for someone to ever get close to that one."

## Shanghai, lights to flag

Hamilton's Ferrari rocketed out of P3 past both Mercedes into Turn 1 — exactly the launch superiority that has defined the SF-26 from the first weekend. Antonelli held his head. He repassed Hamilton at the Turn 14 hairpin on Lap 2 and was never headed again. The defining moment of the race was the Lap 10 safety car for Lance Stroll's stranded Aston Martin. The leaders all pitted: medium to hard for Antonelli, who emerged still in P1 ahead of Colapinto and Ocon. Once Russell had threaded back through the Ferraris, the W17 pace gap to the SF-26 became frightening — close to a second a lap in the final stint. Antonelli ran wide at the Turn 14 hairpin on Lap 52, gathered the car, and crossed the line +5.515 s ahead of Russell.

Only eighteen cars had started the race. McLaren had double-DNS'd it on the formation lap, both Norris and Piastri felled by separate electrical failures on the Mercedes HPP power unit — the reigning constructors' champion arriving in China carrying only fifteen points and leaving with the same. The last time McLaren had failed to start both cars was the 2005 United States Grand Prix at Indianapolis. Hamilton picked up his first Ferrari podium in third, his first podium of any kind since the 2024 Las Vegas Grand Prix.

Antonelli, on the in-lap radio: "I am speechless. I want to cry, to be honest. I said yesterday that I really want to bring Italy back on top, and I did that today." The last Italian to win a Grand Prix was Giancarlo Fisichella in Malaysia in 2006 — five months before Antonelli was born.

## Suzuka, dropped clutch and a Spoon Curve gift

The pole at Suzuka — back-to-back, +0.298 s clear of Russell — was the easy part. Russell admitted on Saturday night that the team had tweaked his set-up before parc fermé and the gamble had not paid. At nineteen years, six months and twenty-five days, Antonelli became the youngest driver in F1 history to take consecutive pole positions.

What happened on Sunday at lights-out is the most expensive launch mistake of his career so far. The wheelspin was viscous. The W17 dropped from P1 to P6 by the apex of Turn 1. Piastri converted P3 on the grid into the lead. Leclerc took P2, Russell P4, Antonelli P6. He told Channel 4 in the pen afterward: "I didn't insert well enough the fingers into the clutch, and that led to the fact that when I dropped it, I didn't have the same angle as well, and I just dropped more than what I should have."

By Lap 2 he had Hamilton. By Lap 4 he had Leclerc. He climbed to P4 and watched Russell ahead of him race Piastri for the actual win. On Lap 21 Russell finally pitted from the lead.

On Lap 22 Oliver Bearman lost his Haas on the grass at Spoon Curve while attempting to pass Colapinto. The closing speed at impact was reported around 308 km/h — a 50G load on the chassis, the kind of accident that ends careers. Bearman was conscious in the car. The safety car came out. Antonelli, yet to pit, dived in for a free stop. Russell, who had pitted one lap earlier, knew before the pit-board went out: "Unbelievable. Wow. F---. Our luck in these last two races."

From the restart Antonelli's second-stint pace was, in his own words, incredible. He pulled 13.7 seconds clear of Piastri to win, set the race fastest lap on Lap 49, and watched Russell get demoted to P4 in the closing laps as Hamilton and Leclerc found a way past. The Bearman safety car had cost the other Mercedes a thirteen-point swing in a single afternoon.

## 19y 7m 4d

*Youngest championship leader in Formula 1 history.*

The previous holder of this record was Lewis Hamilton, who first led the standings at twenty-two years, four months and six days after finishing second at the 2007 Spanish Grand Prix. Antonelli left Suzuka on sixty-three points, nine clear of Russell, sitting in the seat Hamilton vacated to join Ferrari for 2025. The symmetry was not lost on anyone. He had just broken Hamilton's record in Hamilton's old car.

## The five-week silence

On the morning of 13 March 2026, after the United States killing of Iran's Supreme Leader on 28 February and retaliatory Iranian strikes that hit Bahrain among other Gulf targets, F1 and the FIA confirmed neither the Bahrain Grand Prix (12 April) nor the Saudi Arabian Grand Prix (19 April) would take place. Stefano Domenicali called it "a difficult decision… the right one at this stage considering the current situation in the Middle East." Mohammed Ben Sulayem added that "the FIA will always place the safety and well-being of our community and colleagues first." No replacements were inserted. The 2026 calendar dropped from twenty-four races to twenty-two, with a five-week gap between Suzuka and Miami.

Antonelli could decompress. He could train. He could avoid the Italian press, which had already begun comparing him to Alberto Ascari, the last Italian to lead a Formula 1 championship, seventy-three years ago. McLaren and Red Bull could fix what was broken. The pause helped him and his rivals in roughly equal measure, and possibly his rivals a little more.

## Miami, the undercut, the third pole

McLaren arrived in Florida with the upgrade package the five weeks had bought them. Norris took Sprint pole on Friday — the first non-Mercedes pole of the season — and won Saturday's Sprint cleanly from the front. Antonelli, P2 on the Sprint grid, lost six places at the start, was demoted to P6 after a five-second track-limits penalty, and watched the championship gap fall to seven points. Toto Wolff later blamed a glitch on the team's side.

That evening Antonelli answered with one of his best laps of the season: 1:27.798, edging Verstappen by just over a tenth, with Leclerc in P3 and Norris in P4. Russell, struggling on the low-grip surface, qualified P5.

Sunday's start was his best of 2026 and it still cost him places. Verstappen launched from P2 better than Antonelli launched from pole and then ran wide at Turn 1. Leclerc swept through to lead. Antonelli locked up trying to avoid Leclerc's early braking, ran wide, and rejoined P3. By Turn 2 Verstappen had spun on his own and was at the back. Antonelli repassed Leclerc, briefly led, then lost the lead to Norris during the McLaren's electric-strategy stint. The Lap 6 safety car — Hadjar in the wall at Turn 14, Lawson taking Gasly out at Turn 17, the Alpine flipping onto the barrier — set up the pit cycle that decided the race.

Norris led Antonelli by 1.8 s when Mercedes called Antonelli in at the end of Lap 26. McLaren reacted one lap late. Norris pitted on Lap 27, rejoined just behind Antonelli at the pit-exit, and was overtaken into Turn 1 of the next lap as Antonelli used his energy boost on the back straight. He led by 0.4 s on Lap 28, 1.2 s on Lap 29, and Norris never got back inside that gap. There was a gearbox concern from Lap 34 — three or four laps where it refused to upshift cleanly from seventh to eighth — and Norris closed to within 1.4 s with a few laps remaining. Antonelli managed it home. +3.264 s at the flag. Norris afterward: "We just got undercut. There are no excuses other than that. We should've boxed first."

## 3 / 3

*First driver in F1 history to convert his first three pole positions into race victories.*

Damon Hill did it once, taking his first three Grands Prix wins as poles in Hungary, Belgium and Italy in 1993. Mika Häkkinen did it in 1997 and 1998. Antonelli is the only driver in the sport's history to have his first three career poles and his first three career wins overlap exactly.

## −20

*Grid spots lost at the lights, across five 2026 starts.*

He is the only driver on the grid who has lost places at every single start of 2026, Sprint or Grand Prix. Verstappen sits on the same –20 number, but Verstappen's losses are accidents — the Miami spin, the Q1 crash at Albert Park, all incident-driven. Antonelli's are procedural. The 2026 power unit start sequence requires drivers to hold high revs for at least ten seconds before launch to spool the turbo without an MGU-H, and the clutch bite-point window has narrowed. Charles Leclerc, by contrast, has gained twelve places on Lap 1 across four races — the best record in the field. The Ferrari and the Williams (the latter +22 as a team) are leading the constructors in launch performance.

Wolff to Sky Sports Germany after Miami: "Not good at all. That's unacceptable if you want to win a world championship. We just have to dig even deeper to try to understand how we can fix that, because the gaps to Mercedes' rivals are not big enough to cruise into the sunset, and therefore you can't be missing starts." The FIA, meanwhile, is testing a low-power start-detection system that will push Mercedes toward a procedural solution rather than letting them software-patch around it.

## Montreal, twenty clear

The standings after four rounds: Antonelli 100, Russell 80, Leclerc 59, Norris 51, Hamilton 51, Piastri 43. From Round 2 onward, Antonelli has out-qualified Russell at every Grand Prix. Russell's Miami GP qualifying deficit of 0.416 s — on a circuit where he had finished strongly the year before — is the most concerning data point of his season. Wolff to *La Gazzetta dello Sport*: "what we love about this sport is that the clock never lies at the end of the race, and the clock says Kimi deservedly won the last three GPs."

Three of those wins involved race-changing fortune — the Stroll safety car in China, the Bearman safety car in Japan, the Verstappen spin and the McLaren strategy error in Miami. A regression to the mean is statistically due, and Canada is historically a Russell-strong venue. McLaren scored forty-eight of a possible fifty-eight points in Miami after scoring eighteen across the previous three races; if they bring another major upgrade to Imola or Barcelona, the constructors' battle tightens dramatically and Norris's title math starts to look real.

The title is Antonelli's to lose. The W17 is the class of the field. He is leading his team-mate in qualifying and his team-mate in races. The single thing that can take this away from him is the thing that has not been taken away yet: the launch. Twenty grid spots lost across five starts, with eighteen rounds and about 540 points still on the table. If he fixes the start, the championship is his by August. If he doesn't, the records will outlast the lead.

He turns twenty in August, two days before the Hungarian Grand Prix. The clock has not even started.
