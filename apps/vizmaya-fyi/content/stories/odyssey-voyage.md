## A Two-Week Voyage, Ten Years Long

Straight-line, Troy to Ithaca is about 565 nautical miles — a week or two under Bronze Age sail. A modern superyacht does it in a week; a cruise ship in under a day. Homer takes ten years.

The gap between those two facts is the whole story. It is not a story about distance. It is a story about detention — and about the exact point on the map where the real Aegean gives way to an invented Wonderland.

## Ten Years, Almost None of It Sailing

Open the Odyssey expecting a decade of storms and you meet a surprise: of the ten years, only about 127 days are actual voyaging. Everything else is being held. One year with the sorceress Circe on Aeaea. Seven years with the nymph Calypso on Ogygia — nearly three-quarters of the wandering decade in a single place.

Add the two together and roughly eight of the ten years are two stationary islands. The genuinely adventurous sailing amounts to a single season's worth of movement — which is exactly what the explorer Tim Severin proved by doing it, Troy to Ithaca, in three to four months.

## Troy — Where It Begins

Odysseus leaves the sack of Troy with twelve ships and about seven hundred and twenty men. Troy is one of only two undisputed anchors on this whole map: the archaeological mound at Hisarlik in north-west Turkey, whose Late Bronze Age layers align with the Homeric war.

From here east is memory and west is myth. Everything he is about to describe, he narrates himself — a flashback told years later at a foreign court, by a famously unreliable narrator.

## The Cicones

The first landfall is real and barely contested. At Ismaros — the modern Maroneia in Thrace, where a prehistoric acropolis still carries Cyclopean walls — Odysseus sacks the city of the Cicones and spares the priest Maron, who gives him the potent wine he will later use on the Cyclops.

His men linger to feast; Ciconian reinforcements rout them. Six men from every ship are killed — seventy-two in all. The subtraction has begun.

## Cape Malea — Into Wonderland

Rounding Cape Malea, the wind-battered south-east tip of the Peloponnese, a north wind and current beat Odysseus off course past the island of Cythera. This is the hinge of the entire poem.

W. B. Stanford's classic commentary marks the line precisely: "These are the last clearly identifiable places in O.'s wanderings. After this he leaves the sphere of Geography and enters Wonderland." Everything before Malea is mappable. Everything after is contested.

## A Two-Week Trip, in Ten Years

For nine days the north wind carries him — and here the arithmetic turns absurd. The same ~565-mile route that a galley could run in a week, or a cruise ship in a day, becomes a decade.

Even the drift is disputed. A galley under sail makes 70–100 miles a day, which would fling the fleet to Tunisia. But Severin argued a homesick crew fighting to hold position drifts only about thirty — landing them in Libya, roughly due south of Cape Malea. The direction of the wind decides which continent the myth belongs to.

## The Lotus-Eaters

The nine-day blow ends among people who eat a narcotic flowering fruit that erases the desire to go home. A scouting party tastes it and has to be dragged back to the ships by force.

The traditional identification is Djerba, off Tunisia — Polybius's and Strabo's Meninx. Severin puts it in Cyrenaica, eastern Libya. Either way, it is the first stop with no fixed place on any real chart, and the camera has already leapt a thousand miles west.

## The Cyclops

The iconic episode: trapped in the cave of the one-eyed giant Polyphemus, son of Poseidon, Odysseus blinds him with a fire-hardened stake and escapes beneath the giant's own sheep. Then he boasts his real name — and Polyphemus's curse to his father Poseidon becomes the engine that drives the rest of the poem.

Tradition sets it in Sicily, near Mount Etna; the Faraglioni dei Ciclopi sea-stacks at Aci Trezza are pointed out to this day. Bradford preferred western Sicily; Severin, Crete. Six more men are gone.

## Aeolus and the Bag of Winds

Aeolus, keeper of the winds, hosts Odysseus for a month and sends him off with an ox-hide bag holding every wind but the gentle West. For nine days they sail; Ithaca rises on the horizon, close enough to see men tending fires.

Then the crew, certain the bag holds gold, open it. The winds burst free and blow them all the way back. It is the cruelest near-miss in literature — home undone by greed. Tradition places Aeolus at Lipari; Severin at Gramvousa off Crete, whose ancient name Korykos means "leather bag."

## The Laestrygonians

The single greatest catastrophe. The fleet moors inside a narrow, high-walled harbour and a race of cannibal giants hurls boulders down from the cliffs, smashing eleven of the twelve ships with all aboard. Only Odysseus's own vessel, tied up outside the harbour mouth, escapes.

In one afternoon the expedition loses more men than in every other episode combined. Severin found a bay he said matched Homer "exactly" — the circular harbour of Mezapos on the Mani, near Cape Tainaron.

## The Cost of the Voyage

Step back and the voyage reads as a slow arithmetic of loss. Twelve ships and seven hundred and twenty men leave Troy; the Cicones take seventy-two, the Cyclops six, the Laestrygonians eleven whole ships, Scylla six more — and at Thrinacia the last of the crew.

Only the loss events are explicit in the text; the running headcount between them is a reconstruction at about sixty men per ship. However you count it, the destination is the same: one man, alone.

## Circe

On Aeaea the sorceress Circe turns his scouting party into swine. Protected by the herb moly, a gift from Hermes, Odysseus resists, becomes her lover, and stays a full year — the first of the two great detentions. When he finally leaves, she sends him somewhere no living man is meant to go.

Tradition preserves her in the very name of Monte Circeo, a 541-metre promontory rising from the Lazio coastal plain south of Rome.

## The Underworld

Directed by Circe, Odysseus sails to the edge of Ocean, digs a trench, and summons the dead: the prophet Tiresias, who warns him not to touch the cattle of the Sun; his own mother; his fallen comrades.

The most archaeologically resonant candidate is the Necromanteion on the Acheron river in Thesprotia — matching Homer's rivers of the dead. But its excavated ruins date no earlier than the later fourth century BCE, and some scholars now think the building was a fortified farmhouse. Even the best real site here is contested.

## Scylla, Charybdis, and the Sirens

Warned by Circe, Odysseus plugs his crew's ears with wax and lashes himself to the mast to hear the Sirens' song and live. Then comes the strait: on one side the six-headed cliff-monster Scylla, on the other the ship-swallowing whirlpool Charybdis. He hugs Scylla's cliff and loses six men — one to each head — rather than risk the whole ship.

Polybius, in the second century BCE, was the first to fix Scylla and Charybdis at the genuinely treacherous Strait of Messina; the Sirens' isles are the Li Galli, off the Amalfi coast, named for them since antiquity.

## Thrinacia — The Cattle of the Sun

Storms strand the ship for over a month on the island of the Sun. Starving, and against every warning from Tiresias and Circe, the crew slaughter Helios's sacred immortal cattle. Zeus answers with a thunderbolt that splinters the ship. Every last man drowns — except Odysseus.

Tradition reads Thrinacia as Sicily, the three-cornered "Trinakria." He alone survives, clinging to the wreck, drifting back past Charybdis.

## Calypso — Seven Years

The sole survivor washes up on Ogygia, the island of the nymph Calypso, who keeps him seven years and offers him immortality to stay. He refuses it, sitting on the shore each day looking out to sea, until Zeus finally sends Hermes to order his release.

This single stop is the largest block of the entire decade — more than the wars, the monsters and the sailing combined. Tradition places Ogygia at Gozo in Malta, where Calypso's Cave still looks over Ramla Bay; Strabo imagined it far out in the Atlantic. Severin left it, honestly, uncertain.

## The Phaeacians

Freed at last, Odysseus builds a raft and sails seventeen days by the stars, keeping the Great Bear on his left, before Poseidon wrecks him one last time. He washes ashore naked on Scheria and is found by the princess Nausicaa.

It is here, at the court of King Alcinous, that he tells the whole tale you have just followed. The Phaeacians — identified with Corfu since Thucydides — give him treasure and a magical, self-steering ship, and carry him home in a single night's voyage.

## Ithaca — Home

The Phaeacians set the sleeping Odysseus down on Ithaca. Disguised as a beggar by Athena, he reunites with his son, kills the suitors besieging his wife, and reclaims his throne — twenty years after he first sailed for Troy.

Modern Ithaki fits the tradition, backed by Mycenaean-era finds. But the debate is alive: Robert Bittlestone argued Homer's Ithaca is really Paliki, on Kefalonia; and in 2026 James Diggle and John Underhill reframed the question entirely — Homer, Diggle notes, "never actually calls Ithaca an island."

## There Is No True Route

Zoom back out and the honest picture returns. Everything you have just watched fly across the map after Cape Malea is contested. The traditional itinerary loops west to Sicily and Italy; Severin keeps it hugging Greece; the ancient fringe pushed it into the Atlantic. Most classicists today treat the whole of Books 9–12 as imaginary.

Only Troy, Ismaros and the Ithaki–Kefalonia zone rest on excavation. Everything gold on this map is real; everything else is a two-thousand-year-old argument about where a poem happens. Eratosthenes said it best: you will find the scene of Odysseus's wanderings when you find the cobbler who sewed up the bag of the winds.
