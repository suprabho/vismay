# A two-week voyage, ten years long

Odysseus's journey home from Troy is one of the most famous trips in
literature, and one of the shortest. Straight-line, Troy to Ithaca is about
**565 nautical miles** — roughly 650 statute miles, a week or two under
Bronze Age sail. A modern superyacht does it in about a week; a cruise ship in
under a day. Homer takes ten years.

The gap between those two facts is the whole story. It is not a story about
distance. It is a story about **detention**.

## Where the ten years actually go

Open the *Odyssey* expecting a decade of storms and monsters and you will be
surprised by the arithmetic. Of the ten years, only a handful of weeks are
actual voyaging. Everything else is being *stuck*:

- **One year** with the sorceress **Circe** on Aeaea.
- **Seven years** with the nymph **Calypso** on Ogygia — nearly
  three-quarters of the entire "wandering decade" in a single place.

Add the two together and roughly **eight of the ten years** are two stationary
islands. The genuinely adventurous sailing — Troy to the Lotus-Eaters, the
Cyclops, Aeolus, the Laestrygonians, the run to Circe, then the Sirens, Scylla
and Charybdis, and Thrinacia — amounts to a single sailing season's worth of
movement. That is exactly what the explorer Tim Severin demonstrated in 1985
by doing it, Troy to Ithaca, in three to four months.

The poem hides this by being told out of order. Homer opens *in medias res*
with Odysseus already trapped on Calypso's island in year ten; the famous
monster-adventures of Books 9–12 are a **flashback**, narrated by Odysseus
himself at the Phaeacian court — a story within a story, recounted by a
famously unreliable narrator. The map a reader builds is a map drawn from
memory by the man who was lost.

## The hinge: Cape Malea

Up to a point, Odysseus is a real navigator on a real coast. He leaves Troy —
securely identified with the archaeological mound at **Hisarlik** in
north-west Turkey — with 12 ships and about 720 men. He raids the **Cicones**
at **Ismaros**, identified with strong confidence as **Maroneia** in Thrace,
where a prehistoric acropolis still carries Cyclopean walls. He rounds the
southern Peloponnese.

Then, at **Cape Malea** — a genuinely notorious, wind-battered headland near
the island of Cythera — a north wind and current beat him off course, and the
map dissolves. W. B. Stanford's classic 1947 commentary marks the exact line:

> "These are the last clearly identifiable places in O.'s wanderings. After
> this he leaves the sphere of Geography and enters Wonderland."

Everything before Malea is mappable. Everything after is contested. The nine
days of wind that follow are the seam between the known world and myth.

## Rival maps, not one line

There is no single "true" route, and pretending otherwise would be
inaccurate. Instead there are schools:

- The **Traditional / ancient** itinerary (Thucydides, Polybius, Strabo)
  plants most episodes around **Sicily and southern Italy** — the Cyclops near
  Etna, Aeolus at Lipari, Scylla and Charybdis at the Strait of Messina.
- **Victor Bérard** (1929) built the most detailed **western-Mediterranean**
  reconstruction, running all the way to Gibraltar; it became the standard map
  in the 1959 *Atlas of the Classical World*.
- **Ernle Bradford** (1963), a Royal Navy veteran, refined the western route
  from the deck of his own boat.
- **Tim Severin** (1987) argued the opposite: that the whole thing fits the
  **Greek coast**, with landfalls at natural turning points near home — the
  Laestrygonians at Mezapos on the Mani, the Halls of Hades at the Acheron, the
  Sirens around Lefkada. He noted the text is rich in eastern and southern
  place-names and poor in western ones.
- And a **fringe** — Apollodorus, Crates, and Strabo for Ogygia — pushed the
  wanderings out into the **Atlantic**, a reading Polybius refuted using the
  nine-day sail from Cape Malea: it would take far longer than nine days to
  reach the ocean.

Scholars from **Eratosthenes** onward warned the whole exercise may be
futile. His line, preserved by Strabo, is the epigraph this story deserves:

> "You will find the scene of the wanderings of Odysseus when you find the
> cobbler who sewed up the bag of the winds."

The `route-theories` chart shows these as what they are: overlapping clouds of
guesses, spreading west from the Cape Malea pivot. Severin's cluster hugs
Greece; Bérard's reaches Spain; the fringe drifts off the western edge.

## The cost, counted

The voyage is also a slow subtraction. Twelve ships and some 720 men leave
Troy; one man comes home. The losses that are explicit in the text:

- **72 men** to the Cicones (six per ship).
- **6 men** eaten by the Cyclops, Polyphemus.
- **Eleven of the twelve ships**, with all aboard, smashed by the cannibal
  **Laestrygonians** — the single greatest loss.
- **6 men** to **Scylla**, one snatched by each of her six heads.
- **All the rest** at **Thrinacia**, where the starving crew slaughter the
  immortal cattle of the Sun and Zeus destroys the ship with a thunderbolt.
  Only Odysseus survives.

By the time he washes up on Calypso's shore he is alone, and stays alone for
seven years. (The running headcount between those explicit losses is a
reconstruction — Homer gives the loss events, not a per-episode census — so
this story flags the men-remaining line as reconstructed at about 60 men per
ship.)

## Was it ever real?

The honest answer is that the geography is unresolved and probably
unresolvable. Only a few anchors rest on excavation — Troy, Ismaros/Maroneia,
and the Ithaki/Kefalonia zone. Even there the ground is moving. Robert
Bittlestone's *Odysseus Unbound* (2005) argued Homer's Ithaca is really
**Paliki**, the western peninsula of Kefalonia, once a separate island. But in
July 2026 the philologist James Diggle and geologist John Underhill reframed
the whole question: Underhill's geoscience found Paliki was *not* an island in
the Late Bronze Age, and Diggle pointed out something simpler still —

> "Homer has many opportunities to call Ithaca an island, but he never does
> so."

The Necromanteion at the Acheron, long the most archaeologically resonant
candidate for the entrance to Hades, is likewise contested: its ruins date no
earlier than the later 4th century BCE, and some scholars think the structure
was a fortified farmhouse.

## The wine-dark sea

If any single phrase carries the mood of the poem it is the **wine-dark sea** —
Greek *oînops póntos*, "wine-faced" or "wine-looking sea." It appears
seventeen times across the *Iliad* and *Odyssey*, always of the sea (and,
oddly, of oxen). William Gladstone raised the puzzle in 1858: why *wine*, and
not blue? Homer's world has almost no "blue" in it; his wine is only ever red
or black. The debate has run ever since — ancient colour words may have
encoded brightness rather than hue; the sea may genuinely look dark red at
certain light; it may be a linguistic quirk.

For a story about a sea voyage that is really about being kept from home, it is
the right note to end on: a colour we can all picture and none of us can quite
name. The palette this story reads in — deep burgundy through indigo to slate —
is drawn straight from that argument.
