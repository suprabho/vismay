# Ebola 2026: A Vizmaya-fyi Story

**Reporting date:** 29 May 2026
**Outbreak status:** Active PHEIC (declared 17 May 2026)
**Species:** *Orthoebolavirus bundibugyoense* (Bundibugyo virus / BDBV) — no licensed vaccine, no licensed therapeutic

---

## 1. Why this outbreak is different

Three things make the May 2026 outbreak unlike the recent Ebola events that have shaped the public mental model:

1. **It is Bundibugyo, not Zaire.** Of the 50+ Ebola outbreaks since 1976, only **three** have been caused by Bundibugyo virus (Uganda 2007, DRC 2012, DRC/Uganda 2026). The licensed vaccine **Ervebo (rVSV-ZEBOV)** and the licensed monoclonal antibodies **Inmazeb and Ebanga** target Zaire ebolavirus — none is expected to cross-protect against BDBV. For the first time in nearly a decade, responders are facing a major outbreak without a vaccine in the arsenal.
2. **It is in a conflict zone.** The epicenter — Mongbwalu (a gold-mining hub), Rwampara and Bunia in Ituri — sits inside the active operational area of ADF, CODECO and M23. Tedros warned that contact tracing is "nearly impossible while bombs are falling." The roughly **four-week gap** between the index case's symptom onset (24 April) and lab confirmation (15 May) suggests substantial undetected chain-of-transmission.
3. **It reached a regional air hub fast.** Two unrelated confirmed cases turned up in **Kampala within 24 hours** of each other on 15–16 May, both having traveled from DRC. An American physician evacuated to Germany; high-risk contacts moved to Germany and Czechia. The geographic reach is wider than at any equivalent point in the 2018–2020 Kivu epidemic.

WHO declared a **PHEIC on 17 May**, unusually without first convening the Emergency Committee (which then met on 19 May). Africa CDC declared a Public Health Emergency of Continental Security the next day. ECDC activated the EU Health Task Force.

## 3. Country-level context — the historical baseline

The CDC outbreak history shows Ebola is **concentrated in a corridor** running from Guinea/Liberia/Sierra Leone in the west to DRC/Uganda/South Sudan in central-east Africa. Five countries account for over 95% of all human cases since 1976.

| Country | Outbreaks (count) | Cumulative cases | Cumulative deaths | Worst single event |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| **DRC** | 17 | ~4,400+ | ~3,000+ | 2018–2020 Kivu (3,470 cases / 2,287 deaths) |
| **Sierra Leone** | 1 | 14,124 | 3,956 | 2014–2016 West Africa |
| **Liberia** | 1 | 10,678 | 4,810 | 2014–2016 West Africa |
| **Guinea** | 2 | 3,837 | 2,556 | 2014–2016 West Africa |
| **Uganda** | 8 | ~770 | ~330 | 2000 Gulu (425 / 224) |
| **Sudan/South Sudan** | 4 | 335 | 180 | 1976 Nzara/Maridi (284 / 151) |
| **Gabon** | 5 | 268 | 175 | 2001 (65 / 53) |
| **Republic of Congo** | 4 | 249 | 211 | 2003 (143 / 128) |

A few observations worth pulling forward:

- **DRC has had more outbreaks (17) than any other country**, but they have stayed mostly small until 2018. Of the 17, only one has produced more than a thousand cases.
- **The West Africa epidemic dwarfs everything else.** It accounts for **~28,610 of ~46,000+ total human cases** since 1976. Without it, Ebola is mostly a low-thousands-of-cases disease.
- **Uganda's response capability is asymmetrically good.** Eight outbreaks since 2000, all but one contained at single- or double-digit case counts, thanks to UVRI/MoH detection muscle built since 2000 with US CDC support.
- **Cross-border events are real but contained.** Nigeria (20/8) and Mali (8/6) in 2014 are the textbook proof that a single imported case in a major African city can be stopped if the response is fast.

## 4. By species — the picture re-segmented

Every "Ebola" headline hides a species, and the species matters more than the country.

| Species | First seen | Outbreaks | Cases | Typical CFR | Vaccine | Therapeutic |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| **Zaire** | 1976 | 30+ | ~46,000 | 40–90% | **Ervebo (licensed)** | Inmazeb, Ebanga (licensed) |
| **Sudan** | 1976 | 8 | ~672 | 40–50% | Trial candidates only | None licensed |
| **Bundibugyo** | 2007 | **3** | ~290+ | 25–50% | **None** | **None licensed** |
| **Taï Forest** | 1994 | 1 | 1 | N/A | None | N/A |
| **Reston** | 1989 | 7 | ~13 | 0% (asymptomatic) | N/A | Does not cause human disease |

The analytical error that keeps recurring in the press: **conflating species**. The 2014–2016 epidemic and the 2018–2020 Kivu epidemic were Zaire; we have tools for those. The 2022 Mubende outbreak and the Jan 2025 Kampala outbreak were Sudan; we have trial candidates but no licensed product. The 2026 outbreak is **Bundibugyo** — the species we have done the least work on. WHO, IAVI, Oxford/Serum Institute of India (ChAdOx1-BDBV) and Moderna are accelerating trial candidates, but none had begun randomized trials by late May 2026.

## 5. Genomic surveillance — what Nextstrain shows

The Nextstrain BDBV build has been updated with **three new 2026 genomes** (DRC + Uganda), placed against the **34 prior BDBV genomes** from the 2007 Uganda and 2012 DRC outbreaks.

Key finding from initial phylogenetic analysis:
- The 2026 viruses **cluster together** (consistent with a single outbreak)
- They are **genetically distinct from 2007/2012 lineages**
- This supports the interpretation of a **new zoonotic spillover** (probably from a fruit bat reservoir), not human-survivor persistence

For Zaire ebolavirus, the past decade has shown survivor-reactivation lineages causing several DRC outbreaks (2021 Biena, 2021 Beni, the 2021 Guinea outbreak). For BDBV, with only three documented outbreaks and ~15-year intervals, the spillover interpretation is the simpler story.

## 6. Data accessibility — the developer's reality check

A finding worth surfacing for anyone trying to *build* on this story rather than read it: **the authoritative live data is human-readable, not machine-readable.**

| Tier | Source | Format | Live? | Use for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | [WHO Disease Outbreak News](https://www.who.int/emergencies/disease-outbreak-news) | HTML | Per-event | Canonical record |
| 1 | [WHO AFRO weekly bulletin](https://www.afro.who.int/health-topics/disease-outbreaks/outbreaks-and-other-emergencies-updates) | PDF | Weekly | Regional table |
| 1 | [Africa CDC weekly](https://africacdc.org/) | PDF | Weekly | Continental coordination |
| 1 | [ECDC CDTR](https://www.ecdc.europa.eu/en/publications-and-data/monitoring/weekly-threats-reports) | PDF | Weekly | EU-facing synthesis |
| 1 | [US CDC outbreak history](https://www.cdc.gov/ebola/outbreaks/index.html) | HTML | Annual update | Best clean historical table |
| 2 | [ReliefWeb API](https://api.reliefweb.int/v1/reports) | JSON | Daily | Document metadata |
| 2 | [HDX Ebola datasets](https://data.humdata.org/ebola) | CSV (HXL) | Historical | Machine-readable past outbreaks |
| 3 | [cmrivers/ebola](https://github.com/cmrivers/ebola) | CSV | Archived (2015) | 2014–2016 timeseries |
| 3 | [andersen-lab/ebola-drc-epidemiology](https://github.com/andersen-lab/ebola-drc-epidemiology) | CSV | Complete | 2018–2020 Kivu by zone |
| 4 | [Nextstrain BDBV](https://nextstrain.org/) | JSON | Live | Phylogenetic tree |
| 4 | NCBI Virus | API | Live | Sequence downloads |

For a **2026 live tracker**, the practical recipe: scrape WHO DON + AFRO + ECDC weekly, ingest ReliefWeb document metadata, and cross-check at each refresh because **suspected → confirmed reclassification produces downward revisions** that look like errors if you don't handle them explicitly. There is no clean live API.

## 7. What would change the picture

Watch for these triggers in the next few weeks. Each would materially shift risk and response:

- A confirmed case **outside the DRC–Uganda–South Sudan corridor**
- **Sustained local transmission in Uganda** (vs. imported cases only)
- A successful start of the **ChAdOx1-BDBV (Oxford/SII) trial** — they had targeted ~2–3 months from May
- Evidence of **Ervebo cross-protection against BDBV** (limited and currently inconclusive)
- A **second cluster in a major African city** beyond Kampala (Goma is the most-cited concern)

---

## 8. Caveats

- **Numbers move daily** and are anchored here to 26–27 May 2026. The gap between suspected and confirmed counts is unusually large.
- **Conflict-driven undercounting** in eastern DRC almost certainly means reported counts understate true transmission.
- **Source discrepancies are normal:** WHO, US CDC, ECDC and DRC MoH often cite slightly different totals due to cut-off times and reclassification.
- **ProMED reliability** has been questioned since 2022; cross-check against WHO/CDC/Africa CDC before publishing.
