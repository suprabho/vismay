# Fact-Check: Did Indian Government Policy Decisions Disproportionately Benefit the Adani Group?

## TL;DR
- Four of the five claims are **substantially accurate on the verifiable facts** (a rule or clause was changed, after which Adani benefited), but the causal "tailored-for-Adani" inference is **contested**: the government and Adani offer legitimate-sounding rationales, and in several cases Adani was the lowest or highest bidder in a competitive process, or was simply the only firm holding a 100%-export contract.
- The strongest documented cases are the **airports** (finance ministry and NITI Aayog objections overruled; prior-experience requirement dropped) and the **Godda SEZ/power-export** rule changes; the **FCI silo** and **Khavda** cases show real rule changes but also genuine competitive bidding and broader policy context; the **Western Ghats** environmental-clearance claim is **overstated and partly misframed**.
- A consistent pattern recurs across sectors—safeguards proposed by the government's own technical arms were removed and Adani benefitted—which makes "regulatory capture" a reasonable interpretation; but because each case individually has a defensible policy explanation, it remains an interpretation rather than a proven fact.

## Key Findings

**Claim 1 — FCI grain silos: ACCURATE on facts, CONTESTED on causation.** The anti-monopoly clause was real and was dropped after NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs opposed it in a 2022 meeting; Adani Agri Logistics and Leap India together won 110 of 134 contracts (~78% of capacity). But FCI and the government note that awards went to the lowest bidders, that Adani won nothing in Phase II, and that many firms participated.

**Claim 2 — Airports: ACCURATE; the best-documented case.** The prior-experience requirement was removed, no cap on the number of airports was imposed, and a per-passenger fee replaced revenue-share. The DEA and NITI Aayog explicitly objected; their objections were overruled by the PPPAC/Empowered Group of Secretaries. Adani, with zero airport experience, won all six with aggressive bids.

**Claim 3 — Khavda RE land: ACCURATE on the rule change, MISSING CONTEXT on the allocation.** Defence restrictions were relaxed (April–May 2023), SECI surrendered its 23,000-ha parcel, and Adani expanded into the zone. But Khavda is a multi-developer park; Adani's ~19,000 ha / ~9.5 GW is the largest share, yet NTPC, GSECL, GIPCL, SECI and Suzlon's Sarjan Realties also hold large parcels.

**Claim 4 — Godda: ACCURATE, with one factual correction.** The exclusive-export dedicated-line clause was present in the *original* December 2018 guidelines, not a later insertion. But the plant did receive unprecedented SEZ status, Telegraph Act right-of-way powers, and the August 2024 grid-connection amendment that, in practice, benefits only Godda.

**Claim 5 — Western Ghats clearances: OVERSTATED / PARTLY MISLEADING.** The Maharashtra MoU (June 2022, ₹60,000 crore) and fast *preliminary* approvals are real, but the EAC ultimately *rejected/returned* Adani's flagship expansion and imposed extra scrutiny on all Western Ghats pumped-storage projects. The most serious documented red flag is a conflict of interest (an Adani adviser on the appraisal committee), not anomalously fast *final* clearances.

## Details

### Claim 1: FCI Grain Storage Silos

**What happened.** FCI's "Hub and Spoke" silo modernisation programme—a roughly ₹20,000 crore effort to replace conventional godowns with modern steel silos under public-private partnership—proceeded in two phases from 2021. According to a Newslaundry investigation (Astha Savyasachi, 29 May 2026) citing PPP Appraisal Committee files, FCI itself had proposed an "anti-monopoly" clause to stop a single company cornering contracts. In a 2022 meeting, NITI Aayog and the Department of Economic Affairs opposed the restriction, arguing to "let market forces prevail," and the clause was dropped.

**The outcome.** Per the Newslaundry report, Adani Agri Logistics Ltd and Leap India Food & Logistics together "bagged 110 out of 134 silo contracts worth more than Rs 16,500 crore… Around 46.5 lakh metric tonnes (LMT) of grains, out of the total 60, will be stored in silos owned by these two companies"—a concentration the All India Kisan Sabha put at "nearly 77.5%." Adani alone holds contracts worth over ₹9,700 crore. Critics (CPI(M), CPI(ML) Liberation, AIKS) called it a "backdoor" corporatisation of agriculture and demanded reinstatement of the clause and a Joint Parliamentary Committee inquiry.

**Government/Adani rebuttal.** FCI's posted statement (via its official handle, June 2026) rejected the allegation: "It is conclusively established that project awards were determined solely based on competitive bidding outcomes and quoted rates," adding that Adani "secured projects in Phase I but did not win any project in Phase II… demonstrated that there was no preferential treatment," and calling silos a "sunrise sector" where restricting bidding "could have reduced competition." The government separately noted 3–15 bidders competed per project and that Adani's total (~2.82 million tonnes) is "less than half" the tendered capacity, while citing near-zero damage to silo-stored grain.

**Verdict: ACCURATE on the underlying facts (the clause existed, was dropped after NITI/DEA intervention, and a duopoly resulted), but the causal claim of favouritism is CONTESTED.** The competitive-bidding defence is legitimate, and the Phase-II outcome genuinely cuts against a pure-favouritism reading. The strongest surviving critic point: removing an anti-monopoly safeguard predictably enabled concentration regardless of bid mechanics, and that removal was driven by the government's own bodies.

### Claim 2: Airport Privatisation (2018–2019)

**What changed.** For the six-airport round (Ahmedabad, Lucknow, Jaipur, Guwahati, Thiruvananthapuram, Mangaluru), three conditions were set: (1) no prior airport-operation experience required, (2) no cap on the number of airports a single entity could win, and (3) a per-passenger fee replacing revenue-sharing as the bid parameter. The Union Cabinet gave in-principle approval on 8 November 2018; the RFP was floated on 14 December 2018; bids were decided in February 2019.

**The objections.** The Department of Economic Affairs (note dated 10 December 2018) recommended that "not more than two airports" go to a single bidder given the high capital risk, and that spreading awards would enable "yardstick competition." NITI Aayog questioned dropping the prior-experience requirement, warning a bidder "lacking sufficient technical capacity can well jeopardise the project." The DEA note was reportedly not even discussed at the 11 December 2018 PPPAC meeting. The PPPAC ruled that prior airport experience "may neither be made a prerequisite for bidding, nor a post-bid requirement."

**The outcome.** Adani Enterprises—with no prior airport experience—won all six on the highest monthly per-passenger fee, the sole financial criterion. Bids were strikingly aggressive: per Business Standard (25 Feb 2019), "for the Ahmedabad airport, Adani outbid the GMR group by quoting Rs 177 per passenger against the GMR's Rs 85. For the Lucknow airport, Adani bid Rs 171 per passenger beating AMP Capital's quote of Rs 139." Adani later acquired Mumbai (CSMIA) from the GVK Group in 2020 and, with it, the upcoming Navi Mumbai airport.

**Government/Adani rebuttal.** Civil Aviation Minister Hardeep Singh Puri told Parliament that the finance ministry and NITI Aayog were "part of the decision-making process" and that the Empowered Group of Secretaries finalised the bidding contours; the three relaxations were intended to "ensure higher competition and to give more flexibility to bidders." Subhash Chandra Garg (then PPPAC chair) said: "The decisions were taken by the PPPAC… I stand by those decisions, but the decisions were not mine alone." Adani says no rules were violated; ICRA noted the aggressive bids would yield a windfall for AAI (potentially over ₹600 crore/year).

**Verdict: ACCURATE and the best-documented claim.** Primary records (DEA/NITI notes, PPPAC minutes) confirm the rules changed in ways that enabled Adani's entry and clean sweep, and that the government's own technical arms objected and were overruled. The "more competition/flexibility" rationale is genuine, and Adani did bid the most—but the removal of the proposed two-airport cap is precisely what allowed a single entity to take all six.

### Claim 3: Renewable Energy Land in Kutch (Khavda)

**The defence restriction and relaxation.** Land near the India–Pakistan border in the Rann of Kutch was subject to defence construction restrictions. On 21 April 2023, defence-ministry officials met with the Army, the renewable-energy ministry, and Gujarat officials; consensus decisions allowed wind turbines from 1–2 km of the border and hybrid plants from 2–8 km. On 8 May 2023, the defence ministry circulated new guidelines relaxing border-construction rules (extending to borders with Pakistan, China, Bangladesh, Nepal and Myanmar). The Guardian reported that the BJP "lobbied at the highest levels for the protocols to be relaxed to make land in the Rann of Kutch available for both solar and wind construction," and that after rules were relaxed—"making the land significantly more valuable"—it "was transferred from state-run Solar Energy Corporation of India to the Adani Group." Gujarat reportedly wrote to the PMO in April 2023 to raise the matter with the defence ministry.

**The land mechanism.** SECI had been allotted the largest parcel (23,000 ha). Despite attending the April 2023 meeting and receiving the relaxation, SECI sought to *surrender* its 23,000 ha (letter, July 2023), and Gujarat took it back. Adani then expanded into the park, which now spans roughly 445 sq km and targets 30 GW (the wider park footprint is ~72,600 ha).

**Comparative allocation.** Land was allocated to multiple developers: Adani Green ~19,000 ha (~9.5 GW capacity), plus NTPC (~4,750 MW), Gujarat State Electricity Corporation, Gujarat Industries Power Company, SECI, and Suzlon's Sarjan Realties. Adani holds the single largest share but is not the sole developer.

**Government/Adani rebuttal.** The relaxation was framed as a general infrastructure-development policy applying to all borders and all developers, not Adani-specific; the park is a state-nodal-agency (GPCL) project with multiple PSUs participating.

**Verdict: ACCURATE on the rule relaxation and timeline; MISSING CONTEXT on exclusivity.** The defence relaxation, SECI's surrender, and Adani's subsequent expansion are well documented, and the national-security concern flagged by critics is real. But framing Khavda as land simply "handed to Adani" understates that it is a multi-developer park with large PSU parcels. The sharpest unresolved question is *why* SECI surrendered viable land that Adani then used—and the Guardian's documented BJP lobbying to relax the rules that raised the land's value.

### Claim 4: Godda Power Project (Jharkhand)

**(a) Cross-border guideline / December 2018.** Primary-document verification establishes an important correction to a common critic framing: the original "Guidelines for Import/Export (Cross Border) of Electricity-2018" (Ministry of Power OM No. 14/1/2017-Trans, dated 18 December 2018) *already* contained clause 8.9 permitting "Indian Generating Stations supplying electricity exclusively to neighbouring country" to build a dedicated transmission line. A 3 July 2019 addendum changed only clause 5.2(a) (adding gas) and left clause 8.9 untouched, stating "Other contents… remain unchanged." So the exclusive-export enabling clause was original to December 2018, not a later insertion. Godda is, factually, the only Indian plant contracted to export 100% of its output to a neighbouring country.

The consequential later change came on **12 August 2024**, when an MoP office memorandum amended clauses 5.2(a) and 8.9, adding a proviso that the government "may permit connection of such generating station to the Indian Grid… to facilitate sale of power within India in case of sustained non-scheduling of full or part capacity or default notice… including delayed payment under the PPA." This came one week after Sheikh Hasina fled Bangladesh and, in practice, benefits only Godda. Critics (Congress's Jairam Ramesh; Adani Watch/Paranjoy Guha Thakurta) called it bailing out a "crony." On 29 September 2025, the MoP granted Adani Power Telegraph Act (1885) / Section 164 Electricity Act powers to build a Line-In-Line-Out line through 56 villages connecting Godda to the Indian grid.

**(b) Telegraph Act / right-of-way.** Confirmed: the 29 September 2025 order gives Adani Power the same powers as a "telegraph authority" to lay and maintain transmission lines over private property, via Section 164 of the Electricity Act.

**(c) SEZ status.** On 25 February 2019, after the commerce ministry amended SEZ power guidelines (which since February 2016 had explicitly barred standalone power-plant SEZs), Godda became India's first standalone power-sector SEZ, yielding duty waivers, tax exemptions and faster clearances. The Board of Approval for SEZs cleared the enabling amendment on 9 January 2019 "after commerce minister Suresh Prabhu directed it to consider the matter," and approved Adani's proposal on 25 February 2019. Scroll.in (25 Mar 2019) reported: "The government decision to grant SEZ status to the Adani project will save the company billions of rupees in taxes — Rs 3.2 billion annually in clean energy cess alone." (The Washington Post estimated over $1 billion saved on the cess alone over the 25-year PPA.)

**(d) PPA / Bangladesh disputes.** The 25-year PPA (signed November 2017, Adani Power Jharkhand–BPDB, 1,496 MW) became controversial: a leaked copy (December 2022) showed ~$450 million/year in capacity charges regardless of offtake; Bangladesh sought renegotiation over high imported-coal pricing. After the 2024 regime change, Adani halved supply (31 October 2024) over ~$500m+ arrears; Bangladesh's interim government opened a review.

**Government/Adani rebuttal.** Adani Power called the August 2024 amendment "an industry-enabling universal provision aimed at streamlining power export processes without altering existing arrangements," with "no… financial or other obligations on the Government of India or any gains to Adani Power."

**Verdict: ACCURATE, with one factual correction.** The SEZ status, Telegraph Act powers, and the bespoke August 2024 amendment are well documented and unusual; a Jharkhand State Accountant General review (May 2017) had flagged "preferential treatment" and "undue benefits." The correction: the exclusive-export clause was original to December 2018, not tailored later. The "universal provision" defence is undercut by the fact that Godda is the sole beneficiary.

### Claim 5: Accelerated Environmental Approvals (Western Ghats)

**The investment commitment.** On 28 June 2022, the Maharashtra government signed an MoU with Adani Enterprises for five pumped-storage projects (~₹60,000 crore / $7.2 billion) across Tarali, Patgaon, Warasgaon, Malsej Ghat and Koyna in the Western Ghats. (Separately, Adani signed a January 2024 Davos MoU for a ₹50,000 crore data-centre project in Maharashtra—unrelated to the pumped-storage projects, and a common point of conflation.)

**The approvals.** Three of the five projects received "preliminary approval"/Terms of Reference from the central Expert Appraisal Committee (e.g., Tarali ToR in February 2023; preliminary approval in February 2023 for Warasgaon-Warangi). Tarali (1,500 MW) advanced to EIA and a public hearing (March 2024); the EAC recommended its environmental clearance at its 21st meeting (31 December 2024).

**The crucial counter-facts.** This is where the claim weakens. The EAC *rejected/returned* Adani's Warasgaon-Warangi expansion (1,200–1,500 MW) in 2024 over a tripling of the forest-land requirement (24.5 → ~89 ha), and stated that no final approvals for Western Ghats pumped-storage projects would be granted without site visits. The most serious documented impropriety is a **conflict of interest**: an AGEL adviser (Janardhan Chaudhary) was appointed to the reconstituted EAC and attended the 17–18 October 2023 meeting that granted ToR to Adani's Tarali project; the minutes were later amended to claim he had abstained.

**Geography caveat.** These are Western Ghats (Maharashtra) pumped-storage projects, correctly placed. The claim should not be conflated with Adani Green's Khavda solar (Gujarat).

**Verdict: OVERSTATED / PARTLY MISLEADING.** A large investment MoU and fast *preliminary* approvals are real, and the EAC conflict of interest is a genuine governance red flag. But the claim that clearances were anomalously fast collapses partly because the EAC actually *halted/returned* Adani's flagship expansion and imposed extra scrutiny on all Western Ghats projects. "Preliminary approval/ToR" is an early procedural step, not final clearance.

## The Broader Framing: "Regulatory Capture"

The pattern—proposed safeguards removed by the government's own technical bodies (NITI Aayog, DEA, FCI, the defence ministry) followed by Adani benefitting—recurs across all five sectors, which is the core of the "regulatory capture" thesis advanced by analysts and outlets including The Reporters' Collective, Scroll.in, Adani Watch, The Guardian/Financial Times/OCCRP, and opposition politicians (Rahul Gandhi, Jairam Ramesh). The Reporters' Collective (reporters Shreegireesh Jalihal and Kumar Sambhav) has separately documented regulations that let Adani secure coal-era deals previously deemed illegal by the Supreme Court—though its "tailored to fit" framing applies to specific state power tenders rather than the 2018 cross-border guideline.

The 2023 Hindenburg report and a US criminal indictment intensified scrutiny. On the indictment: the five-count case was unsealed on 20 November 2024 in the US District Court for the Eastern District of New York; US Attorney Breon Peace said "the defendants orchestrated an elaborate scheme to bribe Indian government officials to secure contracts worth billions of dollars," with the DOJ alleging Gautam Adani personally offered ~₹1,750 crore (of ~$265m total) to an Andhra Pradesh official to buy 7 GW of SECI power. For context and balance: the DOJ moved to drop the criminal charges with prejudice in May 2026 after Adani pledged a $10bn US investment, and the SEC settled for $18m (Gautam Adani $6m, Sagar Adani $12m)—outcomes that resolved the legal jeopardy without a court finding of guilt.

Adani and the government uniformly deny preferential treatment, emphasising competitive bidding, universal/industry-wide policy framing, and legitimate development goals (storage modernisation, aviation investment, renewable-energy targets, regional energy diplomacy). The group has also pursued aggressive defamation litigation against journalists (RSF documents 10+ cases since 2017), which colours the reporting environment.

## Opposing Perspectives (presented in full)

**The "no favouritism" case:** (1) In every competitive tender, Adani won on price (lowest bidder for FCI silos; highest per-passenger fee for airports). (2) Several rule changes were genuinely universal (the border-construction relaxation applied to all borders; the cross-border export clause predates Godda's controversy). (3) Adani lost where it didn't compete best (FCI Phase II; the EAC rejection of Warasgaon-Warangi). (4) Independent bodies signed off (PPPAC, EGoS with finance-ministry participation). (5) The projects deliver real public goods (grain-storage modernisation, airport investment, renewable capacity, regional energy ties). (6) Concentration partly reflects that few Indian conglomerates have the balance sheet for capital-intensive infrastructure.

**The "regulatory capture" case:** (1) The government's own technical arms repeatedly proposed safeguards (anti-monopoly clauses, airport caps, prior-experience requirements) that were specifically removed before Adani benefited. (2) The cumulative cross-sector pattern is hard to attribute to coincidence. (3) Several changes have a single beneficiary in practice (Godda's August 2024 amendment; the standalone-power SEZ). (4) Conflicts of interest (the Adani adviser on the EAC) and bypassed procedures (the DEA note not discussed; compressed airport timelines) recur. (5) State audits (the Jharkhand AG on Godda) flagged "undue benefits." (6) The pattern coincides with Adani's widely documented closeness to PM Modi.

## Recommendations
- **Treat each claim at its verified level, not at the headline.** Present the airports and Godda cases as the strongest (primary-document trails: DEA/NITI notes, PPPAC minutes, gazette/OM dates, SEZ Board minutes). Use FCI and Khavda as "rule changed + competitive process" cases. Treat the Western Ghats claim cautiously and explicitly correct the "fast clearance" framing.
- **Lead with primary documents:** the 10/11 December 2018 DEA/NITI notes and PPPAC minutes (airports); MoP OM 14/1/2017-Trans of 18 December 2018 and the 12 August 2024 amendment (Godda); the 8 May 2023 defence circular and SECI's July 2023 surrender letter (Khavda); the PPPAC files cited by Newslaundry (FCI); the EAC minutes of 17–18 October 2023 and the 2024 rejection (Western Ghats).
- **Flag what would change the verdicts:** release of the full PPPAC deliberations and the unredacted SECI surrender rationale would most move the airports and Khavda cases; a CAG or JPC inquiry into the FCI silo tenders (demanded by AIKS/CPI) would test the favouritism claim directly; the court-ordered Bangladesh PPA review bears on Godda.
- **Threshold for escalating from "pattern" to "capture":** documentary evidence of direction toward a *specific outcome* for Adani (not merely relaxation of general rules) in two or more cases would justify a firmer "capture" verdict. Absent that, "pattern of favourable rule-making" is the defensible characterisation.

## Caveats
- Several key investigations (the Newslaundry FCI series; parts of the Indian Express airport and Godda reporting) sit behind paywalls or were available to this review only via secondary summary; quoted figures are corroborated across multiple outlets, but the underlying tender/file text was not independently obtained in every instance.
- The "tailored for Adani" claims are allegations and interpretations, not adjudicated findings; no Indian court has ruled the airport, silo, or Godda processes illegal as of this writing, and the US criminal case was dropped (with an SEC civil settlement) without a guilt finding.
- Some sources are openly partisan (opposition parties, Adani Watch, government PIB releases); their factual claims are used only where corroborated, and their characterisations are labelled as such.
- The verbatim text of the 12 August 2024 cross-border amendment was verified via multiple regulatory-tracking reproductions rather than the Ministry of Power PDF directly.
- Some dated events fall in 2025–2026 (the Newslaundry FCI series and FCI's rebuttal; the September 2025 Telegraph Act order; the May 2026 DOJ dismissal) and reflect a fast-moving record that may see further developments.
